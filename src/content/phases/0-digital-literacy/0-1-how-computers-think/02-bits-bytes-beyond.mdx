## Why this matters

You'll see numbers like "256 GB" and "8 MB" everywhere — phone storage, video files, RAM. The relationships between them aren't arbitrary. They come from a simple fact: computers count in twos, not tens.

Once you see the pattern, file sizes stop being magic numbers and start being predictable.

## Powers of two

Every storage size is a power of two. Each step doubles the previous one.

```javascript
console.log(2 ** 10); // 1024
console.log(2 ** 20); // 1048576
console.log(2 ** 30); // 1073741824
```

You will see those exact numbers everywhere — `1024`, `1048576`, `1073741824`. Memorize them by feel, not by digit.

## The kilo confusion

In the everyday world, "kilo" means 1,000. In computer storage, it _used_ to mean 1024 — because 2^10 just happens to be very close to 1,000.

| Word     | Marketing meaning | Real (binary) meaning |
| -------- | ----------------- | --------------------- |
| Kilobyte | 1,000 bytes       | 1,024 bytes           |
| Megabyte | 1,000,000 bytes   | 1,048,576 bytes       |
| Gigabyte | 1,000,000,000     | 1,073,741,824         |

When you buy a 1 TB hard drive and your operating system reports 931 GB, this is why. The drive maker counted in tens; your OS counts in twos.

## What you learned

- Computer storage is measured in powers of two — every step doubles the last.
- A binary kilobyte is 1024 bytes, not 1000.
- Drive marketing uses base 10, which is why advertised sizes look bigger than what your OS reports.
