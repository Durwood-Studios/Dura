## Why this matters

Your computer has two completely different kinds of memory, and they behave nothing alike. One is fast and forgets everything when you unplug it. The other is slow and never forgets. Almost every performance question — "why is this app slow?" — comes back to this distinction.

## RAM: the workbench

RAM is where running programs live _right now_. When you open a browser tab, the page is read from disk into RAM, and all the work happens there. RAM is fast — billions of operations per second — but it's volatile. Pull the plug and it's gone.

Think of it like a kitchen counter. The food in the fridge is your storage. The food on the counter is in RAM, ready to chop, mix, and cook.

## Storage: the fridge

A solid-state drive or hard drive is _persistent_. The file you saved this morning will still be there next week. Storage is much larger than RAM — terabytes vs gigabytes — but it's also much slower. Reading a file from disk into RAM is one of the slowest things a modern computer does.

This is why opening a huge document takes time, and why scrolling through it later feels instant: the slow part was loading it into RAM.

## Addresses

Every byte in RAM has a numeric address, like a house on a street. When you write a number into memory, the CPU isn't asked "remember this number for me" — it's asked "put this number at address 0x7fff5fbff8a0."

You won't usually deal with addresses directly. But when you see a "segmentation fault" or "null pointer," that's the program asking for an address that doesn't belong to it.

## What you learned

- RAM is fast but forgets everything on shutdown; storage is slow but permanent.
- Programs run in RAM. Files live in storage until you open them.
- Every byte of RAM has a numeric address — the CPU works with addresses, not names.
