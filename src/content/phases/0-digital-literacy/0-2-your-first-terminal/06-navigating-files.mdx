## Why this matters

Before you can _do_ things in a terminal, you need to know where you are. Three commands handle nearly all navigation. They are short, ugly, and you will type them so often they become muscle memory within a week.

## pwd: where am I?

`pwd` stands for **print working directory**. It tells you exactly where in the filesystem you currently are.

```text
$ pwd
/Users/dustin/projects/dura
```

A path like `/Users/dustin/projects/dura` is a list of nested folders separated by slashes, starting from the root `/`. Every file and folder on your computer has one of these.

## ls: what's here?

`ls` lists the contents of the current directory.

```text
$ ls
README.md  package.json  src
```

Add `-la` for the long view with hidden files:

```text
$ ls -la
drwxr-xr-x   8 dustin staff   256 Apr  8 10:24 .
drwxr-xr-x   5 dustin staff   160 Apr  8 09:11 ..
-rw-r--r--   1 dustin staff   532 Apr  8 09:11 .gitignore
-rw-r--r--   1 dustin staff  1820 Apr  8 09:30 README.md
```

The leading dots (`.gitignore`, `.`) are hidden files. `.` means "this directory" and `..` means "the parent directory."

## cd: go somewhere

`cd` changes directory. You give it a path.

```text
$ cd src
$ pwd
/Users/dustin/projects/dura/src
$ cd ..
$ pwd
/Users/dustin/projects/dura
```

`cd` with no arguments takes you home. `cd -` takes you back to where you just were. Tab completion fills in long names — start typing and hit `Tab`.

## What you learned

- `pwd` prints where you are. `ls` lists what's here. `cd` moves you somewhere.
- Hidden files start with a dot. `.` is the current dir, `..` is the parent.
- Tab completion is your best friend — use it constantly.
