## Why this matters

The internet feels continuous — you click, the page appears. Behind that smoothness is a much choppier reality: your data is broken into thousands of tiny pieces, sent through dozens of intermediate machines, possibly arriving out of order, possibly some pieces lost along the way. Understanding this changes how you think about every networked bug you'll ever debug.

## Packets

A **packet** is a small chunk of data with a header that says where it's going and where it came from. When you stream a video, your computer isn't receiving "the video" — it's receiving thousands of packets that, when reassembled in the right order, become the video.

Each packet is small (typically around 1500 bytes) so that if one gets lost or corrupted, only a tiny piece needs to be re-sent.

```text
[ from: 142.250.x.x ]
[ to:   192.168.1.5 ]
[ data: ...1500 bytes... ]
```

## Protocols

A **protocol** is just an agreed-upon set of rules for how two computers will talk. Two famous ones:

**TCP** (Transmission Control Protocol) is the careful one. It guarantees packets arrive in order, retries lost ones, and asks the sender to slow down if the receiver is overwhelmed. Most things you use — web pages, email, file downloads — run on TCP.

**UDP** (User Datagram Protocol) is the careless one. It just throws packets and doesn't care if they arrive. This sounds bad until you realize it's exactly what you want for a live video call: by the time you'd notice a missing packet, retransmitting it would be too late anyway. Better to skip it and stay in sync.

## Why buffering happens

When you watch a video, your player downloads packets _ahead_ of where you're currently watching. This buffer protects you from network hiccups: if packets briefly stop arriving, you don't notice because the player has a few seconds in reserve.

When the buffer empties faster than packets refill it, you see the spinner. That spinner is the protocol layer doing its job — waiting for enough data to keep going.

function reassemble(packets) {

.slice()
.sort((a, b) => a.seq - b.seq)
.map((p) => p.content)
.join("");
}

## What you learned

- Data travels as small packets, each with a destination address and a sequence number.
- TCP guarantees in-order, reliable delivery; UDP trades reliability for speed.
- Buffering exists because packets sometimes arrive late or out of order.
