## Why this matters

The transition from 20 to 100 engineers is where startups either become real engineering organizations or collapse under their own weight. At 20 engineers, the CTO can still know every engineer by name, review critical PRs, and make architecture decisions in their head. At 100, none of that is possible. You need management layers, formal processes, and organizational infrastructure that would have been premature at 20 but is now load-bearing.

This transition is especially brutal because the skills that made you successful at 20 — direct involvement in everything, heroic individual contributions, informal decision-making — are the exact skills that will destroy you at 100. You have to let go of being the best engineer in the room and start being the person who builds the system that produces great engineering.

## The management layer

At 20 engineers, you might have two or three team leads reporting to the CTO. At 100, you need managers managing managers. This is the introduction of the director or senior manager layer, and it changes everything.

```
Typical org structure at ~60-100 engineers:

CTO / VP Engineering
├── Director of Product Engineering
│   ├── Team Lead — Buyer Experience (6 engineers)
│   ├── Team Lead — Seller Experience (7 engineers)
│   ├── Team Lead — Payments (5 engineers)
│   └── Team Lead — Search & Discovery (6 engineers)
├── Director of Platform Engineering
│   ├── Team Lead — Developer Platform (5 engineers)
│   └── Team Lead — Data Infrastructure (5 engineers)
└── Engineering Manager — Quality & Enablement
    ├── Team Lead — Security (4 engineers)
    └── Team Lead — QA & Testing (3 engineers)
```

The span-of-control rule of thumb: a manager can effectively manage 5-8 direct reports. More than 8 and one-on-ones get skipped, feedback gets delayed, and career development becomes an afterthought. Fewer than 4 and you have too many layers of management for the value they provide.

## Platform teams become essential

At 20 engineers, shared infrastructure is someone's side project. At 50+, it must be a dedicated team's primary mission. The signals that you need a formal platform team:

```
You need a platform team when:

- Multiple teams are independently solving the same
  infrastructure problems (deployment, monitoring, auth)
- Stream-aligned teams spend >30% of their time on
  undifferentiated heavy lifting
- New team onboarding takes weeks because there is no
  standard way to create and deploy a service
- Production incidents frequently trace to infrastructure
  that nobody owns
- Teams have diverged on tooling — three different CI
  systems, four different logging approaches
```

A platform team at this scale typically owns: CI/CD pipelines, deployment tooling, observability stack (logging, metrics, tracing), service templates and scaffolding, shared libraries, and developer documentation.

The platform team's success metric is not uptime or features shipped — it is **time-to-production for stream-aligned teams**. If a new feature team can go from zero to deployed service in a day, the platform is working. If it takes two weeks, the platform is failing.

## The engineering ladder

At 20 engineers, career progression is informal: "you're doing great, here's more responsibility." At 50+, you need a written engineering ladder — a document that defines levels, expectations, compensation bands, and promotion criteria.

```
A typical IC (individual contributor) ladder:

L1 — Junior Engineer
     Executes well-defined tasks with guidance.
     Scope: individual tasks within a team.

L2 — Engineer
     Owns features end-to-end. Writes production code
     independently. Mentors L1s.
     Scope: features within a team.

L3 — Senior Engineer
     Designs systems. Makes technical decisions that
     affect the team. Leads technical projects.
     Scope: team-level impact.

L4 — Staff Engineer
     Works across teams. Sets technical direction for
     a domain. Identifies and solves problems that
     nobody assigned.
     Scope: multi-team or org-wide impact.

L5 — Principal / Distinguished Engineer
     Sets company-wide technical strategy. Represents
     the company externally. Advises the CTO.
     Scope: company-wide impact.
```

The management track should exist alongside the IC track, not above it. A Staff Engineer and an Engineering Manager should be at comparable levels of seniority and compensation. If management is the only path to higher pay, your best ICs will become mediocre managers.

## Processes that earn their overhead

At this scale, certain processes transition from optional to load-bearing.

**RFC process** — any change that affects multiple teams must be documented in a Request for Comments before implementation. Not for permission. For visibility and feedback. An RFC should take 1-2 hours to write and save weeks of rework.

**Architecture review** — not a gatekeeping committee, but a regular forum (weekly, 1 hour) where teams present designs for cross-cutting changes. The goal is early feedback, not approval.

**Skip-level one-on-ones** — directors and the CTO meet regularly with engineers two levels below them. This is how you detect problems that managers are not surfacing — either because they do not see them or because they are the problem.

**Engineering all-hands** — monthly, 30-60 minutes. Technical wins, architectural direction, headcount plans, Q&A. Transparency prevents the rumor mill from filling information vacuums.

**Incident review** — blameless postmortems for every SEV-1 and SEV-2 incident. Not to assign blame, but to fix systemic issues. More on this in the culture lesson.

## The staff engineer role

Between 50 and 100 engineers, you encounter problems that no single team can solve and that managers should not solve. Migrations that span eight services. Performance problems that cross team boundaries. Architectural decisions that will constrain the company for years. This is where staff engineers earn their title.

```
Staff engineer archetypes (from Will Larson):

Tech Lead    — partners with a team lead, drives the
               technical direction of a critical team

Architect    — responsible for the technical vision of
               a domain that spans multiple teams

Solver       — parachutes into the hardest problems
               across the org, unblocks, moves on

Right Hand   — extends the CTO's reach by representing
               their perspective in rooms they cannot be in
```

A staff engineer is not a senior engineer who writes more code. They are a force multiplier who makes other engineers more effective. Their output is measured in team-level outcomes, not individual commits.

## What you learned

- At 50+ engineers, you need **managers managing managers** with 5-8 direct reports each.
- **Platform teams** become essential when stream-aligned teams spend significant time on undifferentiated infrastructure.
- A written **engineering ladder** with parallel IC and management tracks prevents attrition of top technical talent.
- **Staff engineers** solve cross-team problems that no single team can own and that managers should not own.
- Processes like **RFCs, skip-levels, and incident reviews** earn their overhead at this scale.
