## Why this matters

Every concept in this module — Team Topologies, Conway's Law, scaling transitions, role splits, and culture — converges in the act of designing an org. This is where theory meets the messy reality of real companies with real constraints: budget limits, uneven talent distribution, legacy systems, competing priorities, and humans who have opinions about who they report to.

Org design is not a one-time event. It is a continuous practice. Every quarter, you should evaluate whether your current structure is producing the architecture and delivery velocity your business needs. When it is not, you redesign — deliberately, with Conway's Law as your compass and cognitive load as your constraint.

## The org design process

There is no perfect org chart. There is only the org chart that produces the best outcomes given your current constraints. Here is a repeatable process for designing one.

```
Step 1: Define the target architecture
  What system boundaries do you need?
  What services, platforms, and domains exist?
  Where do you want loose coupling? Tight integration?

Step 2: Map teams to architecture (inverse Conway)
  Each major architectural boundary gets a team.
  Each team should own a deployable unit end-to-end.
  Minimize cross-team dependencies for common work.

Step 3: Classify teams (Team Topologies)
  Which teams are stream-aligned? Platform? Enabling?
  Complicated-subsystem? Label each one explicitly.

Step 4: Define interaction modes
  For each pair of teams that interact:
  Collaboration, X-as-a-Service, or Facilitating?
  Draw the arrows. Make them explicit.

Step 5: Check cognitive load
  For each team: is the scope small enough for 5-9 people
  to hold in their heads? If not, split or offload.

Step 6: Check delivery independence
  Can each stream-aligned team deliver a user story to
  production without waiting on another team? If not,
  move the boundary.

Step 7: Plan the transition
  You cannot reorg overnight. Sequence the changes.
  Move one team at a time. Communicate transparently.
```

## Scenario 1: Series A startup, 25 engineers

A B2B SaaS company has just raised a Series A. The product is a project management tool. The codebase is a Next.js monolith with a PostgreSQL database. There is one team of 25 engineers, a CTO, and no other engineering managers. Deployment takes 45 minutes and requires manual steps.

```
Current state:
- 25 engineers, 1 team, 1 CTO
- Monolith architecture
- Manual deployments
- Product areas: task management, team collaboration,
  reporting & analytics, integrations (Slack, GitHub)
- Growth rate: hiring 3-4 engineers per month

Diagnosis:
- Single team is too large (25 > the 8-person threshold)
- CTO is doing strategy + operations + people management
- Manual deployments are a bottleneck
- Four natural product domains exist

Proposed org:

CTO (technical strategy + interim VPE)
├── Team: Task Management (stream-aligned, 6 engineers)
│   Owns: core task CRUD, views, search, notifications
├── Team: Collaboration (stream-aligned, 6 engineers)
│   Owns: comments, real-time updates, team spaces
├── Team: Reporting (stream-aligned, 5 engineers)
│   Owns: dashboards, analytics, data exports
├── Team: Integrations (stream-aligned, 4 engineers)
│   Owns: Slack, GitHub, webhooks, API
└── Team: Platform (platform, 4 engineers)
    Owns: CI/CD, deployment automation, observability, dev tooling

Interaction modes:
- Stream-aligned teams → Platform: X-as-a-Service
- Stream-aligned teams ↔ each other: Minimal (API contracts)
- Platform → Stream-aligned: Facilitating during onboarding

Next steps:
- Promote 4 tech leads (one per stream-aligned team)
- Hire a VPE within 6 months to take over operations
- Platform team's first mission: automate deployment to < 10 min
```

## Scenario 2: Growth-stage company, 80 engineers

A fintech company processes payments for e-commerce platforms. They have 80 engineers across 10 teams, but the team structure evolved organically and delivery is slowing. There is a CTO and a VP Engineering, but they disagree on priorities. Two "platform" teams have become bottlenecks. Incidents are frequent and postmortems are blame-heavy.

```
Current state:
- 80 engineers, 10 teams, CTO + VPE
- Microservices (30+ services, many tightly coupled)
- Two platform teams that other teams depend on for every deploy
- Monthly incidents, blame-oriented postmortems
- No engineering ladder, no staff engineers
- Three teams working on "payments" with unclear ownership

Diagnosis:
- Conway's Law violation: 3 payment teams = fragmented
  payment architecture (distributed monolith symptoms)
- Platform teams are gatekeepers, not enablers
- No psychological safety (blame postmortems)
- No career ladder = attrition risk for senior ICs
- CTO/VPE misalignment needs explicit role definition

Proposed org:

CTO (technical strategy, external)
VP Engineering (operations, people, delivery)
├── Director — Payments Domain
│   ├── Team: Payment Processing (stream-aligned, 7)
│   ├── Team: Risk & Compliance (complicated-subsystem, 5)
│   └── Team: Merchant Experience (stream-aligned, 6)
├── Director — Platform & Infrastructure
│   ├── Team: Developer Platform (platform, 6)
│   │   Mission: self-service deploy, no more gatekeeping
│   ├── Team: Data Platform (platform, 5)
│   └── Team: Observability (enabling, 4)
│       Mission: embed with product teams to improve monitoring
├── Director — Merchant Products
│   ├── Team: Dashboard & Analytics (stream-aligned, 6)
│   ├── Team: Onboarding (stream-aligned, 5)
│   └── Team: API & Integrations (stream-aligned, 6)
└── Staff Engineers (3, cross-cutting)
    Mission: payment architecture consolidation,
    microservice dependency reduction, incident process

Cultural changes:
- Implement blameless postmortems immediately
- Publish engineering ladder within 60 days
- CTO/VPE write a joint document defining their domains
- Platform teams measured on self-service adoption, not tickets closed
```

## Scenario 3: Early startup, 8 engineers

A developer tools startup has 8 engineers, a technical CEO who codes, and no managers. They build a code review tool. The product has two surfaces: a web app and a CLI. Everyone works on everything, and it mostly works, but two engineers are starting to feel lost.

```
Current state:
- 8 engineers, 0 managers, technical CEO
- Single codebase: web app + CLI + shared backend
- Everyone works on everything
- Two engineers feel overwhelmed by the breadth
- Shipping velocity is still good but starting to slow

Diagnosis:
- Do NOT split into multiple teams yet (8 is borderline)
- The problem is not structure — it is cognitive load on
  two specific engineers who may be junior or new
- Premature team splits at this size create coordination
  overhead that outweighs the benefit

Proposed changes (no reorg):
- CEO assigns informal ownership areas (not teams):
  Web app: 3 engineers, CLI: 2 engineers, Backend: 3 engineers
  (with overlap — people work on multiple areas)
- Pair the overwhelmed engineers with senior engineers
  for 2-week rotations
- Add a weekly 30-minute architecture sync
- Write lightweight ADRs for non-obvious decisions
- Revisit team structure at 12-15 engineers

What NOT to do:
- Do not create team leads (premature hierarchy)
- Do not split into web/CLI teams (premature Conway split)
- Do not introduce sprint planning (overkill at this size)
- Do not hire a VP Engineering (far too early)
```

## The tradeoff matrix

Every org design decision involves tradeoffs. There is no free lunch.

```
Decision                  Pro                     Con
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
More teams               Autonomy, speed          Coordination cost
Fewer teams              Simplicity               Cognitive overload
Platform team            Reduces duplication       Bottleneck risk
No platform team         No bottleneck             Duplicated effort
Tech lead + team lead    Specialized focus         Higher headcount
Combined lead role       Lower cost                Rare skill set needed
Strict team boundaries   Clear ownership           Cross-team friction
Loose team boundaries    Flexibility               Unclear ownership
Central architecture     Consistency               Bottleneck, ivory tower
Distributed decisions    Speed, ownership          Inconsistency
```

The right answer depends on your current constraints, not on best practices from a blog post. A 15-person startup and a 500-person enterprise face different tradeoffs even when they have the same architectural goals.

## What you learned

- Org design follows a repeatable process: define target architecture, map teams, classify by topology, set interaction modes, and check cognitive load.
- **Premature structure** is as dangerous as **missing structure** — match the org complexity to the company stage.
- Every design involves **tradeoffs** — more teams means more autonomy but more coordination cost.
- The **inverse Conway maneuver** is the foundation: start with the architecture you want, then design the org to produce it.
- Org design is **continuous** — revisit team boundaries and interaction modes every quarter.
