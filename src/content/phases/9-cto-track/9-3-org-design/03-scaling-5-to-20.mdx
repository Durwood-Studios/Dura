## Why this matters

Five engineers on a single team communicate through hallway conversations and shared context. Everyone knows the entire codebase. Decisions happen in real time. This is the golden era of startup productivity, and it does not survive the 8-person threshold.

Somewhere between 8 and 12 people, you hit a communication wall. Not everyone can attend the same standup productively. Pull request queues grow. The person who knows how auth works is constantly interrupted. You need to split into multiple teams — and how you make that first split determines whether the next twelve months feel like acceleration or chaos.

This is the most dangerous organizational transition because it is the first one. You have no playbook, no middle managers, and no institutional memory of how to do this. Most CTOs get it wrong the first time. The goal is to get it wrong cheaply.

## When to split

Do not split based on headcount alone. Split when you see these symptoms:

```
Signals that you need to split:

- Standup takes more than 15 minutes and half the updates
  are irrelevant to half the people
- PRs sit in review for more than 24 hours because reviewers
  are overloaded
- Two workstreams are constantly blocking each other
  (shared resources, merge conflicts, deploy queues)
- Knowledge is siloed in one or two people —
  if they leave, critical systems become opaque
- Engineers report feeling "out of the loop" on what
  others are building
```

The trigger is cognitive load, not headcount. A team of 12 working on a single focused product might be fine. A team of 7 working on three unrelated services is already too fragmented.

## How to make the first split

The first split should follow your product's natural seams, not your technology stack. Do not split into "frontend team" and "backend team" — that creates a handoff for every feature. Split by domain.

```
Bad first split (by technology layer):
  Team A: Frontend (React, CSS, browser APIs)
  Team B: Backend (API routes, database, auth)
  Result: Every feature requires coordination between two teams

Good first split (by domain):
  Team A: Core Product (user-facing features, end-to-end)
  Team B: Growth & Onboarding (signup, activation, retention)
  Result: Each team ships features independently

Alternative good split (by user persona):
  Team A: Buyer Experience
  Team B: Seller Experience
  Result: Clear ownership, minimal overlap
```

The litmus test for a good split: can each team deliver a meaningful user story to production without waiting on the other team? If yes, the boundary is in the right place. If every story requires cross-team coordination, move the boundary.

## Tech lead vs team lead

At five engineers, the most senior person is usually the tech lead, the team lead, and the tiebreaker on product decisions — all in one. At two teams, you need to separate these roles. The question is how.

**Tech lead** — responsible for technical direction, code quality, architecture decisions, and unblocking other engineers technically. Writes code. Reviews code. Sets technical standards. Reports to the CTO or VP Engineering on technical health.

**Team lead** — responsible for people, process, and delivery. Runs standups, removes blockers, manages stakeholder communication, tracks velocity. May or may not write code. Reports to the CTO or VP Engineering on team health and delivery.

```
Common patterns at this stage:

Pattern 1: Tech Lead + CTO as team lead
  CTO handles people management for both teams.
  Two tech leads drive technical direction.
  Works until ~15 people, then the CTO drowns.

Pattern 2: Tech Lead / Team Lead split
  One person per team handles both tech and people.
  Simple, but demands a rare combination of skills.
  Works if you have the right people.

Pattern 3: Dedicated Team Leads
  Non-coding team leads manage people and process.
  Tech leads manage architecture and code quality.
  Higher headcount cost, but scales better.
```

There is no universally correct pattern. The wrong answer is leaving it ambiguous. When nobody owns people management, feedback does not happen, career growth stalls, and your best engineers leave.

## The bus factor problem

At five engineers, everyone knows everything. At fifteen, knowledge silos form naturally. The engineer who built the payment integration is the only one who understands it. The person who set up the deployment pipeline is the only one who can fix it when it breaks at 2 AM.

```
Mitigations:

- Pair programming rotations — every engineer works on
  every area at least once per quarter
- Architecture decision records (ADRs) — document the WHY
  behind non-obvious decisions
- On-call rotations that include the whole team, not just
  the author of each system
- Code review across team boundaries — not just within teams
- Internal tech talks — 30-minute sessions where one engineer
  teaches the rest about a system they built
```

The bus factor is not about documentation. Documentation goes stale. The bus factor is about how many people have working mental models of each system. Rotate people through systems, not just through documents.

## Process at this stage

Resist the urge to introduce heavy process. At 5-20 engineers, you need just enough structure to coordinate without enough overhead to slow people down.

```
Minimum viable process (5-20 engineers):

- Weekly team standups (per team, 15 min max)
- Weekly all-engineering sync (30 min, cross-team visibility)
- PR reviews required before merge (1 reviewer minimum)
- Sprint planning every 2 weeks (1 hour per team)
- Monthly retrospectives (per team)
- Quarterly planning (company-level, 2-3 hours)

Not yet needed:
- Formal RFC process (verbal + Slack is fine)
- Dedicated project managers
- Jira (a Kanban board in Linear or GitHub Projects is enough)
- Formal on-call rotations (rotate informally)
- OKRs (too much overhead for this size)
```

Add process only when the absence of that process has caused a real problem twice. Not a theoretical problem. A real one.

## What you learned

- Split teams when **cognitive load symptoms** appear, not at an arbitrary headcount.
- The first split should follow **product domain boundaries**, not technology layers.
- Clarify **tech lead vs team lead** responsibilities explicitly — ambiguity causes attrition.
- Address the **bus factor** by rotating people through systems, not just writing documentation.
- Add process only when its absence has caused a **real problem twice**.
