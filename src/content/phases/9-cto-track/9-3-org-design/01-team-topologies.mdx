## Why this matters

Most org charts are drawn around reporting lines, not around the flow of work. That is why most org charts produce slow software. Matthew Skelton and Manuel Pais formalized what high-performing companies discovered empirically: there are exactly four fundamental team types, and if you use them deliberately, you reduce cognitive load, shrink feedback loops, and ship faster. If you use them accidentally — or not at all — you get the org-chart equivalent of a distributed monolith.

As a CTO, your org chart is the first architectural decision you make each quarter. Get the team boundaries wrong and no amount of technical excellence will save your velocity.

## The four team types

### Stream-aligned teams

A stream-aligned team is aligned to a single, valuable stream of work — a product, a feature area, a user persona, or a business domain. This is the primary team type. Most of your engineering organization should be stream-aligned.

```
Stream-aligned team characteristics:
- Owns a slice of the product end-to-end (frontend, backend, data)
- Has the autonomy to build, test, and deploy without waiting on others
- Measures success by user or business outcomes, not output
- Ideally 5-9 people — small enough for two-pizza conversations
```

The key constraint: a stream-aligned team should not need to hand work off to another team to get something into production. Every handoff is a queue, and every queue is latency.

### Platform teams

A platform team provides internal services, tooling, and infrastructure that reduce the cognitive load on stream-aligned teams. They do not build product features. They build the foundation that makes feature work faster.

```
Platform team examples:
- Internal developer platform (CI/CD, deployment, observability)
- Shared authentication and authorization service
- Data infrastructure (pipelines, warehousing, self-serve analytics)
- Cloud infrastructure and Kubernetes management
```

A good platform team operates like a product team whose customers are other engineers. They have an API, documentation, SLAs, and a roadmap driven by internal customer feedback — not by what they find technically interesting.

The anti-pattern: a platform team that becomes a bottleneck, requiring tickets and approvals for every change. If stream-aligned teams are waiting on the platform team more than they are building, the platform is failing.

### Enabling teams

An enabling team helps stream-aligned teams acquire new capabilities. They are consultants, not builders. They work alongside a stream-aligned team temporarily, transfer knowledge, and move on.

```
Enabling team examples:
- A security team that embeds with product teams to adopt threat modeling
- A performance team that teaches profiling and optimization techniques
- A data science team that helps product teams integrate ML models
- An accessibility team that audits and trains on WCAG compliance
```

The critical distinction: enabling teams have an expiration date on each engagement. If an enabling team is permanently embedded with a stream-aligned team, it has become part of that team — formalize it or fix the dependency.

### Complicated-subsystem teams

A complicated-subsystem team owns a component that requires deep specialist knowledge — so deep that most stream-aligned engineers cannot reasonably maintain it. These teams are rare and should stay rare.

```
Complicated-subsystem team examples:
- Video codec / real-time media processing
- Mathematical optimization engine (route planning, pricing algorithms)
- Hardware driver or firmware interface layer
- Compiler or language runtime
```

The test for whether you need one: if the subsystem requires more than six months of domain study for a competent engineer to contribute safely, it probably warrants a dedicated team. If a motivated generalist could ramp up in a few weeks, fold it into a stream-aligned team.

## Cognitive load as the design constraint

The entire Team Topologies framework rests on one principle: **minimize the cognitive load on each team**. Every team has a finite capacity for complexity. When you pile too many responsibilities, technologies, or domains onto a single team, quality drops and delivery slows — not because people are lazy, but because human working memory is limited.

```
Three types of cognitive load:

Intrinsic    — the complexity inherent in the problem domain
              (e.g., financial regulations, video encoding)

Extraneous   — the complexity imposed by tooling and process
              (e.g., manual deployments, flaky CI, bad APIs)

Germane      — the complexity that produces learning and growth
              (e.g., designing a new architecture, learning a framework)

Platform teams reduce extraneous load.
Enabling teams convert extraneous load into germane load.
Complicated-subsystem teams isolate intrinsic load.
Stream-aligned teams focus on germane and intrinsic load.
```

When a stream-aligned team is struggling, the first question is not "do they need more people?" It is "what cognitive load can we remove?"

## Interaction modes

Teams do not exist in isolation. Skelton and Pais define three interaction modes between teams:

**Collaboration** — two teams work closely together on a shared goal for a bounded period. High communication bandwidth, high discovery, but high coordination cost. Use this when exploring a new domain or building a new integration.

**X-as-a-Service** — one team provides a capability that another consumes through a well-defined API or interface. Low coordination cost, clear ownership boundaries. This is the steady-state for platform teams.

**Facilitating** — one team coaches another. The enabling team helps the stream-aligned team build a capability, then steps back. The goal is independence, not permanent dependency.

## What you learned

- **Stream-aligned teams** own a product slice end-to-end and should be the majority of your org.
- **Platform teams** reduce extraneous cognitive load by providing self-service internal tooling.
- **Enabling teams** temporarily coach stream-aligned teams to acquire new capabilities, then step back.
- **Complicated-subsystem teams** isolate deep specialist knowledge that most engineers cannot reasonably maintain.
- The primary design constraint is **cognitive load** — not headcount, not technology, not reporting lines.
