## Why this matters

Nobody enters management hoping to fire someone. But avoiding this responsibility when it is necessary harms the person underperforming (who deserves honest feedback and a chance to course-correct or find a better fit), harms the team (who carries the extra load and resents the lack of accountability), and harms you (who loses credibility as a leader). This is the hardest thing you will do as a manager. It should always feel hard. The day it feels easy, something has gone wrong with you. But feeling hard does not mean avoiding it.

## Before the PIP: the feedback trail

A PIP should never be a surprise. If the first time someone hears they are underperforming is the PIP document, you have failed as a manager. The trail should look like this:

**Month 1-2: Verbal feedback in 1:1s.** "I've noticed X. The expectation is Y. Let's talk about how to close the gap." Document the conversation in your notes. Be specific — "your code quality needs improvement" is useless. "Your last three PRs required significant rework in review, specifically around error handling and edge cases" is actionable.

**Month 2-3: Written follow-up.** Send an email or Slack message after the 1:1 summarizing the feedback, the expected improvement, and the timeline. "As we discussed, I need to see improvement in X by [date]. Here's what success looks like." This creates a paper trail and removes ambiguity.

**Month 3-4: Formal PIP if no improvement.** The PIP is a structured document with specific, measurable goals, a defined timeline (typically 30-60 days), and clear consequences. It should be created in partnership with HR.

## Anatomy of a PIP

A good PIP is a genuine attempt to help someone succeed. A bad PIP is a paper trail designed to justify a predetermined termination. Your team will know the difference, and so will the person on the PIP.

**Required elements:**

- **Specific performance gaps.** Not "needs to improve communication." Instead: "Must proactively communicate blockers within 24 hours rather than waiting until standup."
- **Measurable success criteria.** "Complete three PRs per sprint that pass review with no more than one round of requested changes."
- **Support offered.** Pairing sessions, reduced scope, mentorship, training budget. You are saying "I want you to succeed and here's how I'll help."
- **Timeline.** 30 days for clear-cut performance issues. 60 days for behavioral or complex gaps.
- **Consequences.** "If the criteria are not met by [date], we will part ways." Unambiguous.

Check in weekly during the PIP. Document every check-in. If the person is improving, acknowledge it explicitly. If they are not, say so clearly. Do not let the PIP expire without a clear conversation about where they stand.

## The termination conversation

If the PIP ends without sufficient improvement, or if the situation warrants immediate termination (ethics violations, harassment, fraud), the conversation itself matters enormously.

**Logistics:** Have HR present. Hold it in a private room (or a private video call if remote). Schedule it early in the day, early in the week. Do not fire someone on a Friday afternoon — they will spend the weekend spiraling with no access to HR or support.

**The conversation itself should be short — under 10 minutes.** This is not a negotiation. The decision has been made.

1. "I need to let you know that we've decided to end your employment, effective [date]."
2. Brief, honest reason. "Despite the work we did during the improvement plan, the performance gaps we discussed were not resolved."
3. Logistics. "HR will walk you through severance, benefits continuation, and next steps."
4. Human acknowledgment. "I know this is difficult. I want you to know that I respect the work you've done here and I wish you well."

**Do not:**

- Apologize excessively. It centers your discomfort, not their experience.
- Relitigate the performance history. The PIP trail is the record.
- Make promises about references you cannot keep.
- Let the conversation become a debate. The decision is final.

## Legal basics

You are not a lawyer, and this is not legal advice. But you need to know the landscape.

**Document everything.** Every piece of feedback, every 1:1 note, every PIP check-in. In the absence of documentation, terminations become legal risk.

**Consult HR and legal before every termination.** No exceptions. Even when it seems straightforward.

**Understand at-will employment.** In most US states, employment is at-will — either party can end it at any time. But "at-will" does not mean "without consequence." Wrongful termination claims arise from discrimination, retaliation, or breach of implied contract.

**Protected classes exist.** You cannot terminate someone because of their race, gender, age, disability, religion, national origin, sexual orientation, or other protected characteristics. If a termination even appears to correlate with a protected class, you have a problem.

**Severance is optional but wise.** Offering 2-4 weeks of severance in exchange for a release agreement is standard practice. It provides the departing employee a cushion and provides the company legal protection.

## When firing is the kindest option

This sounds counterintuitive, but keeping someone in a role where they are failing is not compassion. It is avoidance. The person on a PIP knows they are struggling. Their confidence erodes daily. Their relationship with the team deteriorates. Every week you delay is a week they could be spending somewhere they would thrive.

Kim Scott's Radical Candor framework describes "ruinous empathy" — being so concerned about someone's feelings that you withhold the truth they need. Sometimes the truth is: "This is not the right role for you, and both of us know it."

## Team impact

The team is watching. They know who is underperforming. They have been carrying the load. How you handle the departure tells them everything about your leadership.

**After the termination:**

- Brief the team promptly. "As of today, [name] is no longer with the team. I can't share details about the circumstances, but I want to be available for any questions about how this affects our work."
- Do not badmouth the departed person. Ever. The team will wonder what you say about them when they are not in the room.
- Acknowledge the workload impact and adjust sprint commitments immediately.
- Check in individually with people who were close to the departed team member.

## What you learned

- A PIP should never be a surprise — it must be preceded by verbal feedback, written follow-up, and clear documentation of the performance gap
- The termination conversation should be short, direct, and humane — under 10 minutes, with HR present, and never on a Friday
- How you handle departures defines your leadership credibility — brief the team promptly, never badmouth, and adjust workload immediately
