## Why this matters

As an IC, your relationship with your manager was the most important one in your work life. As an EM, you now have two equally critical relationships: down to your team and up to your director (or VP). Most new managers focus entirely downward and neglect the upward relationship. The consequence is predictable: your team does great work that nobody above you knows about, your resource requests get denied because you did not build the case, and you get blindsided by organizational decisions that you could have influenced if you had been in the room.

Managing up is not politics. It is the professional skill of ensuring your team's work is visible, your needs are understood, and your judgment is trusted by the people who make resource and priority decisions.

## Status reports your director wants

Your director manages 4-8 teams. They do not have time to parse a wall of bullet points. They need three things from your status report:

**1. What shipped this cycle and its impact.** Not "completed 14 tickets." Instead: "Shipped password reset flow — reduces support tickets by an estimated 200/week." Tie output to outcomes.

**2. What is at risk and what you are doing about it.** Surprises destroy trust upward faster than anything else. If a project is slipping, say so before your director hears it from someone else. But never present a problem without a plan. "The search migration is two weeks behind due to an undocumented API limitation. We've identified a workaround and expect to recover one of those weeks. I'll have an updated timeline by Friday."

**3. What you need from them.** An additional headcount. A decision on a competing priority. Air cover for pushing back on a cross-team request. Be explicit. Your director cannot help you if they do not know what you need.

**Format:** Keep it under one page. Use the same structure every time so your director can scan it in 60 seconds. Send it at the same time each week. Consistency builds trust.

## Skip-level meetings

Skip-levels are meetings between your director (or their boss) and your direct reports. They can feel threatening — "why does my boss need to talk to my team without me?" — but they are a healthy organizational practice when done well.

**Why they exist:** Senior leaders need unfiltered signal from the front lines. They want to know if the team is healthy, if priorities make sense on the ground, and if there are problems that the EM layer is not surfacing (intentionally or not).

**How to handle them:**

- Do not coach your reports on what to say. This backfires spectacularly when discovered.
- Tell your reports what to expect. "Director Chen does skip-levels quarterly. They'll ask about your experience on the team, what's working, what isn't. Be honest — that's the point."
- Ask your director afterward if there is anything you should know. Most directors will share themes without breaking individual confidence.
- If something concerning surfaces, treat it as data, not as a threat.

## Managing expectations

Under-promise, over-deliver is cliche because it works. The expanded version:

**Set expectations early.** When a project starts, communicate the timeline, the risks, and the assumptions. "We can deliver the MVP in six weeks assuming no changes to scope and no major incidents. If requirements shift, the timeline shifts."

**Update expectations proactively.** Do not wait for the weekly status meeting to flag a slip. The moment you know something has changed, communicate it. A two-day delay communicated immediately is a minor adjustment. A two-day delay discovered at the deadline is a trust violation.

**Manage the narrative.** If you do not frame the story, someone else will. When your team ships something significant, make sure your director knows about it in a way they can relay upward. When a setback happens, own it with context. "We missed the deadline because we discovered a data integrity issue that would have caused customer-facing bugs. We chose to fix it rather than ship broken. Here's the new timeline." That is a very different story than "they missed the deadline."

## When to escalate

Escalation is not failure. It is the appropriate use of organizational hierarchy to resolve issues that cannot be resolved at your level.

**Escalate when:**

- Two teams disagree on priority and neither has the authority to break the tie
- A risk exceeds your authority to mitigate (budget, headcount, timeline commitment to a customer)
- You have raised an issue with a peer and it has not been resolved after two attempts
- A decision needs to be made faster than the normal process allows

**How to escalate:**

1. Tell the other party you are escalating. "I'm going to bring this to Director Chen because we need a tiebreaker and neither of us has the authority." Never go around someone without telling them.
2. Present both sides fairly. Your credibility depends on being trusted as an honest broker.
3. Recommend a path forward. "Here are the two options. I recommend option A because [reasons]. But I wanted your input before we commit."

## How to say no to your boss

You will be asked to commit to things you cannot deliver. Saying yes to everything and failing is worse than saying no with a clear explanation.

**The no framework:**

- Acknowledge the ask. "I understand this is a priority."
- State the constraint. "My team is at capacity with the search migration and the Q3 reliability commitments."
- Offer an alternative. "We could take this on if we deprioritize the reliability work, or we could start it next sprint. Which would you prefer?"
- Let them decide. You are giving your director the information they need to make a tradeoff, not refusing to cooperate.

This is not insubordination. This is what your director hired you to do — provide ground truth about capacity and help them make informed decisions.

{`// Executive Status Report Generator
// Models the structured format that directors can scan
// in 60 seconds and get the signal they need.

function generateStatusReport(data) {
// data: {
// team, week,
// shipped: [{ item, impact }],
// atRisk: [{ item, reason, mitigation, newTimeline }],
// needs: [{ ask, context, urgency }],
// metrics: { velocityActual, velocityTarget, teamHealth }
// }

const report = [];

// Header
report.push("STATUS: " + data.team + " | Week of " + data.week);
report.push("=".repeat(50));

// Section 1: Shipped
report.push("\\nSHIPPED THIS WEEK");
if (data.shipped.length === 0) {
report.push(" (no releases this week — mid-sprint)");
} else {
data.shipped.forEach(s => {
report.push(" [DONE] " + s.item);
report.push(" Impact: " + s.impact);
});
}

// Section 2: At Risk
report.push("\\nAT RISK");
if (data.atRisk.length === 0) {
report.push(" (nothing flagged)");
} else {
data.atRisk.forEach(r => {
report.push(" [RISK] " + r.item);
report.push(" Why: " + r.reason);
report.push(" Mitigation: " + r.mitigation);
if (r.newTimeline) report.push(" New timeline: " + r.newTimeline);
});
}

// Section 3: Needs
report.push("\\nNEEDS FROM YOU");
if (data.needs.length === 0) {
report.push(" (no blockers needing escalation)");
} else {
data.needs.forEach(n => {
const urgencyTag = n.urgency === "high" ? "[URGENT] " : "";
report.push(" " + urgencyTag + n.ask);
report.push(" Context: " + n.context);
});
}

// Section 4: Pulse
report.push("\\nTEAM PULSE");
report.push(" Velocity: " + data.metrics.velocityActual + " / " +
data.metrics.velocityTarget + " points");
const healthEmoji = data.metrics.teamHealth >= 4 ? "Strong" :
data.metrics.teamHealth >= 3 ? "Stable" : "Needs attention";
report.push(" Health: " + healthEmoji + " (" + data.metrics.teamHealth + "/5)");

return report.join("\\n");
}

const report = generateStatusReport({
team: "Platform Engineering",
week: "June 16, 2025",
shipped: [
{ item: "Password reset flow (v2)", impact: "Estimated -200 support tickets/week" },
{ item: "Rate limiter on public API", impact: "Blocks abuse pattern affecting 3 enterprise customers" }
],
atRisk: [
{
item: "Search migration",
reason: "Elasticsearch 8.x API breaking changes not in vendor docs",
mitigation: "Found workaround, rewriting adapter layer this week",
newTimeline: "2 weeks delayed (was June 27, now July 11)"
}
],
needs: [
{
ask: "Decision on Q3 reliability vs. new feature priority",
context: "Product wants notification system in Q3. Infra wants us on reliability. Cannot do both.",
urgency: "high"
}
],
metrics: { velocityActual: 28, velocityTarget: 30, teamHealth: 3.5 }
});

console.log(report);`}

## What you learned

- Managing up is not politics — it is ensuring your team's work is visible, your needs are understood, and surprises never travel upward
- Status reports should be scannable in 60 seconds: what shipped (with impact), what is at risk (with mitigation), and what you need
- Saying no to your boss is a professional skill — present the tradeoff and let them decide which priority to sacrifice
