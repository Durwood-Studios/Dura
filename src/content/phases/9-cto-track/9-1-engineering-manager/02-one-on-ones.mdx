## Why this matters

The 1:1 is the single highest-leverage meeting on your calendar. It is where trust gets built, where problems surface before they become crises, and where your reports feel heard or ignored. Most managers either skip 1:1s ("we talk enough in standup") or run them as status updates ("what are you working on?"). Both approaches waste the opportunity. A well-run 1:1 program is the difference between a manager who is surprised by resignations and one who sees them coming months in advance.

## Cadence and logistics

**Weekly, 30 minutes, never canceled.** This is the baseline. You can go to 45 minutes for senior reports who need more coaching bandwidth, but never biweekly. Biweekly 1:1s signal that the relationship is not a priority, and one cancellation means you go a month without connecting.

Block them at consistent times. Your report should be able to plan around the 1:1. Tuesday at 2pm, every week, same room or same video link. Consistency builds the habit of preparation on both sides.

**Their meeting, not yours.** The 1:1 belongs to your direct report. They set the agenda. If they show up with nothing, that itself is a data point — either they are disengaged, they do not trust the space, or they need coaching on how to use it. Your job is to create the conditions where they bring the real stuff, not just the safe stuff.

## The two types of 1:1

**Project 1:1s** focus on the work. Blockers, technical decisions, timeline concerns, cross-team dependencies. These are necessary but should not dominate. If every 1:1 is a project 1:1, you are running a standup with extra steps.

**Career 1:1s** focus on the person. Growth trajectory, skill gaps, job satisfaction, team dynamics, long-term goals. Schedule these explicitly — at least one per month should be a dedicated career conversation. Ben Horowitz writes in _The Hard Thing About Hard Things_ that a manager who only discusses tasks is a project manager, not a people manager.

Alternate between the two, and always leave room for the unplanned. The most important things your report tells you will come in the last five minutes, when they finally feel safe enough to say it.

## Questions that open doors

The opening question sets the entire tone. "What's on your mind?" — from Michael Bungay Stanier's _The Coaching Habit_ — is the single best opener because it hands control to your report and signals that the space is theirs.

**For building trust:**

- "What's on your mind?"
- "What is the most useful thing I can do for you this week?"
- "Is there anything you would change about how the team works?"

**For surfacing problems:**

- "What is frustrating you right now?"
- "If you were me, what would you change?"
- "Is there anything you are not saying because you think I don't want to hear it?"

**For career growth:**

- "Where do you want to be in two years, and what skills are you missing?"
- "What work are you doing that you want to do more of?"
- "What work are you doing that you want to do less of?"

**For checking engagement:**

- "On a scale of 1-10, how happy are you here? What would move it up one point?"
- "When was the last time you felt really proud of something you shipped?"

## Red flags to watch for

Silence is a red flag. If a report consistently has "nothing to discuss," something is wrong. Either they do not trust the space, they are checked out, or they are interviewing elsewhere. Dig gently.

Repeated complaints about the same issue mean you failed to act the first time. Track what comes up and follow through. Nothing destroys trust faster than asking for feedback and then ignoring it.

Sudden cheerfulness from someone who was previously frustrated is often a sign they have mentally checked out — they stopped fighting because they stopped caring. Or they accepted an offer. Either way, pay attention.

## Building trust over time

Trust is not built in a single conversation. It is built through consistent behavior over months. Show up on time. Follow through on commitments. Share context from leadership that you are allowed to share. Admit when you do not have the answer. Defend your team publicly when they deserve it.

Lara Hogan's "Manager Voltron" framework emphasizes that different reports need different things from their manager — some need autonomy, some need visibility, some need coaching. Your job is to figure out which one each person needs and provide it. The 1:1 is where you learn that.

## Try it

<SandboxExercise language="javascript">
{`// 1:1 Template Generator
// Builds a structured agenda based on the type of 1:1
// and the report's current signals.

function generateOneOnOneAgenda(config) {
// config: { type, reportName, weeksSinceLast1on1, recentSignals }
// type: "project" | "career" | "check-in"
// recentSignals: array of "disengaged" | "frustrated" | "thriving" | "new-role" | "post-incident"

const agenda = {
report: config.reportName,
type: config.type,
opening: "What's on your mind?",
sections: [],
closingQuestion: "Is there anything else you want to discuss that we haven't covered?"
};

// If it's been more than 1 week, acknowledge the gap
if (config.weeksSinceLast1on1 > 1) {
agenda.sections.push({
topic: "Reconnect",
questions: [
"It's been " + config.weeksSinceLast1on1 + " weeks — what have I missed?",
"What changed since we last talked?"
],
timeMinutes: 5
});
}

// Add type-specific sections
if (config.type === "career") {
agenda.sections.push({
topic: "Growth & trajectory",
questions: [
"Where do you want to be in two years?",
"What skills are you actively building right now?",
"What work energizes you vs. drains you?"
],
timeMinutes: 15
});
} else if (config.type === "project") {
agenda.sections.push({
topic: "Current work",
questions: [
"What's blocking you right now?",
"Any technical decisions you're uncertain about?",
"How confident are you in the current timeline?"
],
timeMinutes: 10
});
}

// Signal-responsive sections
const signals = config.recentSignals || [];

if (signals.includes("frustrated")) {
agenda.sections.push({
topic: "Frustration check",
questions: [
"I've noticed some friction — what's driving it?",
"If you could change one thing about the team right now, what would it be?"
],
timeMinutes: 10,
priority: "high"
});
}

if (signals.includes("disengaged")) {
agenda.sections.push({
topic: "Engagement pulse",
questions: [
"On a scale of 1-10, how are you feeling about work?",
"When was the last time you felt proud of something you shipped?"
],
timeMinutes: 10,
priority: "high"
});
}

// Calculate total time
const totalMinutes = agenda.sections.reduce((sum, s) => sum + s.timeMinutes, 0) + 5;
agenda.estimatedMinutes = totalMinutes;
agenda.fitsIn30Min = totalMinutes <= 30;

return agenda;
}

// Example: career 1:1 with a frustrated report
const agenda = generateOneOnOneAgenda({
reportName: "Alex",
type: "career",
weeksSinceLast1on1: 1,
recentSignals: ["frustrated"]
});

console.log("1:1 Agenda for:", agenda.report);
console.log("Type:", agenda.type);
console.log("Opening:", agenda.opening);
agenda.sections.forEach(s => {
console.log("\\n[" + s.timeMinutes + "min]", s.topic + (s.priority ? " (HIGH)" : ""));
s.questions.forEach(q => console.log(" -", q));
});
console.log("\\nEstimated:", agenda.estimatedMinutes, "min | Fits in 30:", agenda.fitsIn30Min);`}

## What you learned

- Weekly 1:1s should never be canceled and belong to your direct report — their agenda, not yours
- Alternate between project-focused and career-focused 1:1s, with at least one dedicated career conversation per month
- Red flags include consistent silence, repeated unresolved complaints, and sudden mood shifts — all signal trust or engagement problems
