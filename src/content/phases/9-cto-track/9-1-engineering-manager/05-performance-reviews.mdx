## Why this matters

Performance reviews are where trust compounds or collapses. A good review tells someone exactly where they stand, where they are growing, and what they need to do next. A bad review is a surprise that destroys months of relationship-building. Most managers write reviews the week they are due, pulling examples from the last month because that is all they remember. The result is a document that reflects recency bias rather than reality. You can be the manager whose reviews people actually value — but it requires a system, not a last-minute sprint.

## Feedback vs. evaluation

These are two different activities, and conflating them is the root of most performance review dysfunction.

**Feedback** is ongoing, informal, and forward-looking. "Here's what I noticed. Here's how to improve." It happens in 1:1s, after code reviews, during project retrospectives. Feedback should be continuous — waiting for review season to share it is managerial malpractice.

**Evaluation** is periodic, formal, and backward-looking. "Here is where you stood relative to expectations for your level over the past cycle." It determines compensation adjustments, promotion decisions, and formal records.

The problem arises when evaluation is the only time feedback happens. If someone hears "you're not meeting expectations" for the first time in a written review, you failed to give feedback when it mattered. Julie Zhuo's _The Making of a Manager_ makes this point directly: reviews should contain no surprises. Every piece of evaluative content should have been discussed in a 1:1 first.

## Documenting throughout the cycle

You will not remember what someone did six months ago. Neither will they. The fix is simple but requires discipline.

**Keep a running brag doc for each report.** After every sprint, jot down 2-3 bullet points per person. Shipped features, mentorship moments, incident response, cross-team collaboration, areas that need improvement. This takes five minutes per person per sprint. Across a six-month cycle with six reports, that is roughly three hours of note-taking that saves you twenty hours of panicked recall during review week.

Some managers use a shared doc where the report also adds their accomplishments. This is collaborative and removes the information asymmetry where your report did great work you did not see.

**Tag entries by competency.** If your company's engineering ladder evaluates on technical skill, execution, communication, and leadership, tag your notes accordingly. When review time arrives, you filter by competency and the review writes itself.

## Writing reviews that drive growth

**Structure each competency section as:**

1. **Assessment.** One sentence: where they are relative to their level.
2. **Evidence.** Two to three specific examples from the cycle. Not "you shipped good code" but "your redesign of the payment retry logic reduced failed transactions by 30% and your RFC was the clearest technical document the team produced this half."
3. **Growth area.** One concrete, actionable improvement. Not "improve communication" but "practice presenting technical tradeoffs to non-technical stakeholders — volunteer to present the Q3 architecture review to product."

Avoid the "feedback sandwich" (positive-negative-positive). Adults see through it, and it dilutes both the praise and the criticism. Lead with the overall assessment, then address strengths and growth areas directly.

**Quantify where possible.** "Shipped 12 PRs" is less useful than "took ownership of the most complex migration in the cycle and delivered it ahead of schedule with zero production incidents." Impact matters more than volume.

## Calibration sessions

Calibration is where a group of managers review and adjust performance ratings to ensure consistency across teams. Without calibration, one manager's "exceeds expectations" is another manager's "meets expectations."

**How calibration works:**

- Each manager presents a brief case for each rating, especially for anyone above or below "meets expectations."
- The group challenges ratings that seem inconsistent. "You rated Jordan as 'exceeds' for shipping three features, but Priya shipped four and you rated her as 'meets.' What's the difference?"
- Ratings are adjusted based on the discussion to create a consistent bar across the organization.

**Your job in calibration** is to advocate for your reports with specific evidence. "Jordan should be rated 'exceeds' because they led the incident response for the three-hour outage in October, mentored two junior engineers who are now operating independently, and delivered the search refactor under a compressed timeline." Facts win in calibration. Feelings do not.

## Building a promotion case

A promotion case is not "they've been at this level for two years." It is evidence that someone is already operating at the next level consistently.

**The test:** If they were hired tomorrow at the proposed level, would anyone be surprised? If yes, they are not ready. If no, you have your case.

**Structure a promotion packet as:**

- **Summary:** One paragraph on why they are ready.
- **Evidence by competency:** Three to five examples per ladder dimension showing next-level work sustained over at least two quarters.
- **Peer feedback:** Quotes from collaborators (cross-team signal is especially powerful).
- **Gap acknowledgment:** What they still need to grow into at the new level. This shows maturity in the assessment and builds credibility.

Start building the packet 1-2 cycles before you plan to submit it. Identify the gaps early and create opportunities for your report to demonstrate the missing competencies.

## What you learned

- Feedback (ongoing, forward-looking) and evaluation (periodic, backward-looking) are different activities — reviews should contain zero surprises
- A running notes system with 2-3 bullets per report per sprint eliminates recency bias and makes review season a synthesis exercise instead of a recall exercise
- Promotion cases require evidence of sustained next-level performance across multiple competencies, not tenure or hours worked
