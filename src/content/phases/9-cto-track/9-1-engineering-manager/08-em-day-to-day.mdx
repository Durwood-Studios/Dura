## Why this matters

The biggest shock for new engineering managers is Monday morning. You open your calendar and it is a wall of colored blocks from 9am to 5pm. Standup, 1:1, 1:1, sprint planning, cross-team sync, hiring debrief, 1:1, leadership meeting, incident review. Between these blocks are 15-minute gaps that are too short for meaningful work but too long to ignore. This is your life now. The question is not how to eliminate the meetings — many of them are genuinely necessary. The question is how to design your week so that the meetings serve their purpose, the gaps become useful, and you still have the energy to be present for the people who need you.

## Calendar anatomy of a real EM week

Here is what a typical week looks like for a manager with six direct reports and two active projects:

**Monday:** Standup (15m), sprint planning or grooming (60m), 1:1 with director (30m), cross-team sync (30m). Roughly 2.5 hours of meetings. The rest is catching up on weekend Slack messages, reviewing the week ahead, and triaging anything urgent.

**Tuesday-Thursday:** Three 1:1s per day (90m total), standup (15m), one or two ad-hoc meetings (design review, incident debrief, hiring sync). Roughly 3-4 hours of meetings per day. The remaining time goes to reviewing PRs (if you still do light code review), writing performance feedback, preparing for calibrations, and the operational work of management — responding to escalations, unblocking dependencies, updating project trackers.

**Friday:** Standup (15m), sprint retro or demo (60m), one or two 1:1s. Aim to keep Friday afternoon clear for thinking, writing, and next-week prep. This is when you write your status report, review your running notes, and reflect on whether the week's time allocation matched your actual priorities.

If this sounds like a lot of meetings, it is. The job is meetings. The skill is making them count.

## Meeting hygiene

Bad meetings are not a law of nature. They are a design failure. Every meeting on your calendar should pass three tests:

**1. Does this need to be a meeting?** Information sharing rarely does. A Loom video, a Slack post, or a written update often works better and respects async time. Meetings are for discussion, decisions, and relationship-building — things that require real-time interaction.

**2. Does this need me?** You do not need to attend every meeting you are invited to. "I'll skip this one — send me the notes and ping me if you need a decision" is a complete sentence. Delegate attendance to a senior IC when appropriate — it also gives them visibility.

**3. Is the time right?** A 30-minute meeting scheduled in the middle of a three-hour block destroys the block. Batch meetings together. If you have five meetings on Tuesday, try to make them consecutive so the rest of the day has usable blocks. Cal Newport's "time-block planning" approach is worth studying here.

**Every meeting should have:** An agenda (even if it is one bullet point), a defined duration (default to 25 minutes instead of 30 to create buffer), and a clear outcome — a decision made, an action item assigned, or a relationship strengthened.

## Energy management

Time management is necessary but insufficient. You also need to manage energy. Not all hours are equal.

**High-energy hours** (usually morning) go to your most important work: difficult 1:1s, performance conversations, strategic thinking, writing. Do not waste your peak hours on email triage.

**Low-energy hours** (post-lunch, late afternoon) go to routine meetings, administrative tasks, and information consumption. Schedule your standup and status syncs here.

**Recovery time is not optional.** Block 30 minutes after every emotionally demanding meeting (a PIP check-in, a difficult 1:1, a firing conversation). You cannot be present for your 3pm 1:1 if you are still processing your 2pm conversation. A walk, a coffee, silence — whatever resets you.

**The danger of empty space.** New managers feel guilty about unbooked time. They fill gaps with more meetings or more Slack monitoring. Resist this. Unstructured time is where you notice patterns, think about team health, and prepare for the conversations that matter. Lara Hogan calls this "manager energy" — the capacity to be thoughtful rather than reactive.

## The maker-manager tension

You were a maker. You are now a manager. The transition is not complete until you accept that your schedule will never look like a maker's schedule again.

But your team is still made up of makers. And every meeting you schedule with an engineer fragments their day. This creates a tension: you need information from your team, and they need uninterrupted time to build.

**How to resolve it:**

- Batch all team ceremonies into one or two days. If standup, grooming, and retro all happen on Monday and Friday, Tuesday through Thursday are largely meeting-free for the team.
- Protect "no-meeting blocks" on the team calendar. Many companies do "No Meeting Wednesday" or "Focus Fridays." Enforce it. If someone schedules over the block, push back.
- Use async-first communication for status and information sharing. Move updates to written form and save synchronous time for discussion and decisions.
- Shield your team from meetings they do not need to attend. Your job is to be the interface between the team and the organization. If a cross-team sync only needs one representative, you go — or send a senior IC who wants the exposure.

## Protecting your team's time

This is the EM's shield function. Your team should rarely feel organizational overhead directly. You attend the leadership meetings so they do not have to. You translate vague executive priorities into clear team goals. You absorb the political friction so your engineers can focus on engineering.

This means you will sometimes be in meetings that feel pointless to you personally. That is the cost of the shield. The value is a team that ships consistently because their time is respected and their context is clear.

**Practical shields:**

- Turn down meeting invites that include your entire team when only one person needs to attend
- Summarize leadership decisions in a weekly Slack post rather than cascading meetings
- Negotiate deadlines on behalf of your team rather than passing pressure through
- Say "let me check with the team" instead of committing their time in real-time

## What you learned

- An EM's week is 50-70% meetings by design — the skill is not eliminating them but making each one earn its time through clear agendas, appropriate duration, and defined outcomes
- Energy management matters as much as time management — schedule high-stakes conversations during peak hours and build recovery buffers after emotionally demanding meetings
- The EM's shield function means absorbing organizational overhead so the team can focus — attend meetings they do not need to, translate executive priorities into clarity, and protect no-meeting blocks
