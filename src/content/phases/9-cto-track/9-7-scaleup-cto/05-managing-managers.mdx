## Why this matters

At a 50-person engineering org, you manage engineers directly. At 100+, you manage the people who manage engineers. This is a fundamentally different skill. When you managed engineers, your feedback loop was tight — you saw the code, you saw the work, you saw the problems in real time. Now your information is filtered through a layer of managers, each with their own biases, blind spots, and communication styles. Your ability to maintain organizational health depends on how well you develop, calibrate, and trust your managers.

## The leverage shift

Managing 8 engineers directly, you influence 8 people. Managing 4 engineering managers who each manage 8 engineers, you influence 32 people — but only if your managers are effective. A weak manager does not just underperform individually. They suppress the output of their entire team. A team of 8 engineers under a weak manager might produce the output of 4. Under a strong manager, the same 8 engineers produce the output of 10.

Your highest-leverage activity is developing your managers. Every hour you invest in making an engineering manager 10% better pays dividends across their entire team.

## Skip-level 1:1s

Skip-level meetings are your direct connection to the people doing the work. They are not a way to undermine your managers — they are a way to verify that your managers are creating healthy environments.

### Structure

Meet with each skip-level report once per month for 30 minutes. At 100 engineers with 4 managers, that is about 24 skip-level 1:1s per month — roughly 6 per week, manageable but not trivial.

### What to ask

Do not ask "how is your manager doing?" directly. That puts people in an uncomfortable position. Instead, ask questions that reveal management quality indirectly:

- "What are you working on, and do you feel like it's the right priority?"
- "When you were last blocked, how did it get resolved?"
- "Do you feel like you're growing in your role? What would help?"
- "Is there anything happening on the team that concerns you?"
- "What's one thing you'd change about how your team operates?"

### What to listen for

**Healthy signals**: the engineer knows why their work matters, feels supported, has a growth plan, trusts their manager to advocate for them.

**Unhealthy signals**: the engineer is confused about priorities, feels micromanaged or ignored, has not had a career conversation in months, is afraid to raise concerns with their manager.

When you hear unhealthy signals, do not intervene directly. Coach the manager. "I heard in a skip-level that priorities feel unclear on your team. What's your take on that?" Give them the signal without revealing the source, and let them fix it.

## Calibration

Calibration is the process of ensuring that performance standards are consistent across teams. Without calibration, one manager's "exceeds expectations" is another manager's "meets expectations." Engineers talk. They will notice, and it will corrode trust.

### How to calibrate

Bring your managers together quarterly. Each manager presents their team's performance assessments. The group discusses and adjusts until standards are aligned.

**Rules for calibration sessions**:

- Every rating must have specific evidence. "She's great" is not evidence. "She led the migration project that reduced latency by 40%, mentored two junior engineers, and resolved a P0 incident in under an hour" is evidence.
- Managers advocate for their people, but accept group calibration. If the group says a rating is too high, the manager must either provide stronger evidence or adjust.
- Calibration is confidential. What is said in the room stays in the room.
- Document the outcomes. Managers need a clear record of what was decided and why, so they can communicate consistently to their reports.

### Common calibration problems

**Grade inflation**: every manager rates everyone as "exceeds expectations." This makes the rating system meaningless. Fix it by requiring a distribution — not a forced stack rank, but a guideline. If 80% of your org "exceeds expectations," your expectations are too low.

**Recency bias**: the manager only remembers the last two weeks. Fix it by requiring managers to keep running notes throughout the quarter.

**Halo effect**: a strong performer gets top marks in every dimension, even dimensions where they are average. Fix it by evaluating each competency independently with specific examples.

## Coaching your managers

Most first-time engineering managers were promoted because they were strong individual contributors. They were not trained to manage. Your job is to train them.

### Weekly 1:1s with your managers

These are the most important meetings on your calendar. Each one should cover:

- **Their team**: what is going well, what is struggling, who needs attention
- **Their decisions**: what trade-offs they are weighing, where they need input
- **Their growth**: what management skills they are developing, where they feel stuck

### Common coaching conversations

**The manager who cannot delegate**: "You approved every PR this week. What would happen if you only reviewed the ones that touch the payment system?" Help them define what requires their attention and what can be trusted to senior engineers.

**The manager who avoids conflict**: "You mentioned that an engineer has been underperforming for three months. What feedback have you given them?" Walk through the conversation they need to have. Role-play it if necessary. Conflict avoidance does not protect the engineer — it deprives them of the feedback they need to improve.

**The manager who over-indexes on technical details**: "You spent your 1:1 debugging a query together. That's IC work. What did you learn about their career goals, their morale, their blockers?" Redirect them from doing the work to enabling the work.

**The manager drowning in meetings**: "Show me your calendar. Which of these meetings would survive if you had to cut 30%?" Help them ruthlessly prioritize. A manager with no time to think is a manager who reacts instead of leads.

## When to intervene

You coach through managers, not around them. But some situations require direct intervention:

- **Ethical violations**: harassment, discrimination, retaliation. You do not coach through these. You act immediately.
- **Attrition crisis**: if a team loses three engineers in a quarter, the manager may need to be moved, not coached.
- **Strategic misalignment**: if a manager is leading their team in a direction that contradicts company strategy, and coaching has not corrected it, you make the call directly.

Every other situation — and there are many — is a coaching opportunity. The goal is managers who do not need you to solve their problems. That is the same enabler mindset from lesson one, applied one level up.
