## Why this matters

At a startup, technical strategy is whatever the founding engineers decide over lunch. At a scale-up, technical strategy is a document that 200 people reference when making decisions you will never see. The difference between a CTO who scales and one who becomes a bottleneck is whether their technical vision lives in their head or in a system that operates without them.

## The 1-year technology roadmap

A technology roadmap is not a list of features — the product team owns that. A technology roadmap is a prioritized plan for the technical capabilities, infrastructure investments, and architectural changes that enable the product roadmap.

### Structure

Divide the roadmap into three horizons:

**Now (0-3 months)**: concrete projects with owners, milestones, and expected outcomes. "Migrate the user service to the new authentication system. Owner: Auth team. Milestone 1: shadow traffic by March 15. Milestone 2: full cutover by April 30. Expected outcome: eliminate the legacy auth service and reduce login latency by 200ms."

**Next (3-6 months)**: planned initiatives with clear scope but flexible timelines. "Implement distributed tracing across all services. Expected outcome: reduce MTTR for cross-service incidents by 50%."

**Later (6-12 months)**: strategic bets with defined goals but undefined solutions. "Evaluate edge computing for latency-sensitive workloads. Goal: sub-100ms response times for the real-time collaboration feature."

### What belongs on the roadmap

- Platform investments (observability, CI/CD, developer tooling)
- Architecture migrations (monolith decomposition, database migrations, API versioning)
- Reliability improvements (SLO targets, disaster recovery, capacity planning)
- Security initiatives (compliance requirements, pen test remediation, zero-trust adoption)
- Technology adoption (new languages, frameworks, or infrastructure components)

### What does not belong

Feature work. Product timelines. Sprint plans. The technology roadmap informs the product roadmap by describing what is technically possible and when. It does not duplicate it.

## The RFC process

An RFC (Request for Comments) is a written proposal for a significant technical decision. "Significant" means: it affects multiple teams, it is hard to reverse, or it sets a precedent that future decisions will follow.

### When to write an RFC

- Adopting a new language, framework, or major dependency
- Changing the architecture of a core system
- Introducing a new data store or communication pattern
- Defining a standard that all teams must follow
- Any change that will take more than 2 person-months to implement

### RFC template

```markdown
# RFC: [Title]

**Author**: [name]
**Status**: Draft | In Review | Accepted | Rejected | Superseded
**Date**: [date]
**Reviewers**: [list]

## Problem

What problem are we solving? Who is affected? What is the cost of not solving it?

## Proposal

What are we proposing? Be specific about the approach, not just the goal.

## Alternatives considered

What other approaches did we evaluate? Why were they rejected?

## Trade-offs

What are we giving up? What risks are we accepting?

## Migration plan

How do we get from here to there? What is the rollback plan?

## Timeline

How long will this take? What are the phases?

## Open questions

What do we not know yet? What feedback are we seeking?
```

### The review process

1. Author publishes the RFC to the engineering-wide channel
2. Two-week comment period — any engineer can comment
3. Designated reviewers (architecture council or domain experts) must approve
4. Author addresses feedback, updates the RFC, and presents at the architecture council
5. Architecture council accepts, rejects, or requests revision

The key principle: **anyone can comment, but not everyone can block.** If every engineer has veto power, nothing gets approved. Designate 3-5 reviewers per RFC. Their approval is required. Everyone else's feedback is advisory.

## The architecture council

An architecture council is a small group (5-7 people) of senior technical leaders who review RFCs, set technical standards, and ensure architectural consistency across teams.

### Who sits on it

- CTO (chair, but does not dominate)
- VP/Director of Engineering
- 2-3 principal or staff engineers from different domains
- 1 rotating seat for a senior engineer (gives broader visibility and develops future leaders)

### What they do

- **Review RFCs**: approve, reject, or request changes. Meet biweekly to discuss open RFCs.
- **Maintain the technology radar**: a living document that categorizes technologies into Adopt, Trial, Assess, and Hold. Teams reference this when choosing technologies.
- **Resolve cross-team technical disputes**: when two teams disagree on an approach, the council mediates.
- **Set standards**: coding standards, API design guidelines, data governance policies. Published in the engineering handbook.
- **Conduct architecture reviews**: before a team begins a major initiative, they present their design to the council for feedback. Not approval gatekeeping — early feedback that prevents expensive mistakes.

### What they do not do

- Approve individual PRs or design choices within a team's domain
- Dictate implementation details
- Override a team's technical lead on team-scoped decisions

The council sets the guardrails. Teams operate freely within them.

## Architecture Decision Records

Every significant technical decision should be recorded as an Architecture Decision Record (ADR). Unlike RFCs, which are proposals, ADRs are historical records of decisions already made.

```markdown
# ADR 0023: Use PostgreSQL for the event store

**Date**: 2025-09-15
**Status**: Accepted
**Context**: We need a persistent event store for the new event-sourcing architecture.
We evaluated PostgreSQL, EventStoreDB, and Apache Kafka.
**Decision**: Use PostgreSQL with a dedicated events table and NOTIFY/LISTEN
for real-time subscriptions.
**Rationale**: The team already has deep PostgreSQL expertise. EventStoreDB
would require new operational knowledge. Kafka is overkill for our current
event volume (< 10K events/second).
**Consequences**: We accept that PostgreSQL may become a bottleneck if event
volume exceeds 100K/second. We will revisit this decision at that threshold.
```

ADRs answer the question that new engineers always ask: "Why did we build it this way?" Without them, the answer is archaeology — digging through old PRs, Slack threads, and tribal knowledge. With them, the answer is a five-minute read.

## Communicating strategy

A strategy that lives in a document nobody reads is not a strategy. Communicate it repeatedly through multiple channels:

- **All-hands presentation**: once per quarter, present the technology roadmap to the full engineering org. Connect every initiative to a business outcome.
- **Written strategy doc**: published in the engineering handbook, updated quarterly. This is the canonical reference.
- **Team-level translation**: each engineering manager should translate the roadmap into what it means for their team's priorities.
- **Decision reference**: when making any technical decision, ask "does this align with the roadmap?" If it does not, it needs an explicit exception with justification.

Strategy without communication is a thought exercise. Strategy with communication is organizational alignment.
