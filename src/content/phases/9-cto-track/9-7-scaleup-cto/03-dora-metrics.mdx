## Why this matters

"How is your engineering team doing?" is a question every CTO hears from the board, the CEO, and themselves. Without data, the answer is a feeling. Feelings are unreliable. The DORA research program (DevOps Research and Assessment), backed by years of peer-reviewed studies across thousands of organizations, identified four metrics that reliably predict engineering and organizational performance. They are the closest thing the industry has to a ground truth for engineering effectiveness.

## The four metrics

DORA divides engineering performance into two axes: **throughput** (how fast you ship) and **stability** (how safe your shipments are). Two metrics on each axis.

### Deployment frequency

**What it measures**: how often your team deploys code to production.

**Why it matters**: frequent deployments mean small batch sizes. Small batches are easier to review, easier to test, easier to roll back, and easier to understand when something breaks. Organizations that deploy multiple times per day have lower change failure rates than organizations that deploy monthly — counterintuitive until you realize that a 50-line change is inherently less risky than a 5,000-line release.

**Elite benchmark**: multiple deploys per day.

**Common failure mode**: measuring deployments without distinguishing meaningful changes from config tweaks. A deployment that ships a new feature and a deployment that updates an environment variable are not equivalent. Track both, but do not conflate them.

### Lead time for changes

**What it measures**: the time from a developer's first commit to that code running in production.

**Why it matters**: long lead times signal process bottlenecks. If code sits in review for three days, waits for a release train for five days, and then goes through a two-day QA cycle, you have a ten-day lead time regardless of how fast your engineers write code. Lead time exposes friction that velocity metrics hide.

**Elite benchmark**: less than one day.

**Where to measure**: start the clock at first commit, not at "ticket picked up." You want to capture the full pipeline from code-written to code-in-production.

```
First commit → PR opened → Review complete → Merged → Deployed
    |              |              |             |          |
    |---- coding --|-- review ----|-- merge ----|-- deploy-|
    |                                                      |
    |------------- lead time for changes ------------------|
```

### Change failure rate

**What it measures**: the percentage of deployments that cause a failure in production — a service degradation, an outage, a rollback, or a hotfix.

**Why it matters**: this is the stability counterpart to deployment frequency. Shipping fast means nothing if 30% of your deployments break something. Elite teams deploy more often and break less, because their small batches, automated tests, and deployment infrastructure catch problems before users do.

**Elite benchmark**: 0-15%.

**What counts as a failure**: any deployment that requires a rollback, a hotfix within 24 hours, or causes a user-facing incident. Be honest with the definition. Teams that narrow the definition to hide failures learn nothing.

### Mean time to recovery (MTTR)

**What it measures**: how long it takes to restore service after a production failure.

**Why it matters**: failures are inevitable. The question is not "will production break?" but "how fast can you fix it?" Organizations with low MTTR can afford to take more risks with deployments because the blast radius of any failure is contained by the speed of recovery. This is the metric that makes deployment frequency safe.

**Elite benchmark**: less than one hour.

**How to reduce it**: automated rollbacks, feature flags, blue-green deployments, comprehensive runbooks, and practiced incident response. Every minute of MTTR is a minute you can engineer away with better tooling.

## The performance tiers

The DORA research identifies four performance clusters:

| Metric              | Elite        | High           | Medium             | Low              |
| ------------------- | ------------ | -------------- | ------------------ | ---------------- |
| Deploy frequency    | Multiple/day | Weekly-monthly | Monthly-biannually | Biannually+      |
| Lead time           | < 1 day      | 1 day - 1 week | 1 week - 1 month   | 1-6 months       |
| Change failure rate | 0-15%        | 16-30%         | 16-30%             | 16-30%           |
| MTTR                | < 1 hour     | < 1 day        | < 1 day            | 1 week - 1 month |

Elite performers are not trading speed for stability. They have both. That is the key insight: throughput and stability are not opposites. The practices that increase one (CI/CD, automated testing, trunk-based development, observability) also increase the other.

## Implementing DORA tracking

### Start with what you have

You do not need a vendor tool to track DORA metrics. Start with data you already collect:

- **Deployment frequency**: count production deployments from your CI/CD system. GitHub Actions, GitLab CI, and most deployment platforms log every deployment with a timestamp.
- **Lead time**: measure the time between the first commit on a branch and the merge-to-main timestamp (or the deployment timestamp if you have it).
- **Change failure rate**: tag incidents to the deployment that caused them. Divide incidents by total deployments per period.
- **MTTR**: measure from incident-opened to incident-resolved in your incident tracking system.

### Avoid gaming

The moment you tie DORA metrics to performance reviews or bonuses, teams will game them. A team can inflate deployment frequency by splitting every change into ten trivial PRs. They can reduce change failure rate by narrowing the definition of "failure." They can reduce lead time by merging without review.

DORA metrics are diagnostic tools, not targets. Use them to identify bottlenecks and track improvement trends. Never use them to rank teams against each other.

### Look at trends, not snapshots

A single week's numbers are noise. Look at 90-day rolling averages. You are looking for directional movement: is lead time shrinking quarter over quarter? Is change failure rate stable as deployment frequency increases?

## What DORA does not measure

DORA metrics tell you how well your delivery machine works. They do not tell you whether you are building the right things. A team can have elite DORA metrics while shipping features nobody uses. Pair DORA with product metrics — adoption, retention, revenue impact — to get the full picture.

DORA also does not measure developer satisfaction, code quality, or technical debt. A team burning out to hit elite deployment frequency is not performing well. Use DORA alongside developer experience surveys and code health indicators for a complete view of engineering effectiveness.

## The CTO's role

You do not compute these metrics yourself. You create the expectation that they are tracked, reviewed monthly, and acted upon. When lead time spikes, you ask the right questions: is it a review bottleneck, a test bottleneck, or a deployment bottleneck? When change failure rate climbs, you fund the reliability investment. When MTTR is too high, you authorize the observability tooling.

DORA metrics are the instrument panel. You are the pilot, not the mechanic.
