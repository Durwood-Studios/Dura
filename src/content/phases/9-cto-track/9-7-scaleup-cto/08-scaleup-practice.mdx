## Why this matters

This is the capstone of the Scale-Up CTO module. Everything you have learned — enablement, process design, DORA metrics, platform engineering, managing managers, technical strategy, and growth challenges — converges here. You are designing an engineering organization for a 200-person company. Not writing code. Not reviewing PRs. Designing the system that produces the software.

## Scenario

You are CTO of a company that has grown from 30 to 200 employees in 18 months. Engineering is now 80 people and will be 120 by year-end. The codebase is a monolith with two satellite services. Deployment is weekly. The founding engineers are burned out. Three engineering managers were promoted from IC roles in the last six months with no management training. The CEO asks you: "What does engineering look like in 12 months?"

This lesson walks through how you answer that question.

## Organizational structure

### Conway's Law is your constraint

Conway's Law states that organizations produce systems that mirror their communication structures. This is not a suggestion — it is an observation that holds with remarkable consistency. If you have three teams, you will get a three-part architecture. If a team owns two services, those services will be tightly coupled.

The **inverse Conway maneuver** uses this deliberately: design the org structure to produce the architecture you want, not the other way around.

### Team topologies

Structure your engineering org around four team types:

**Stream-aligned teams** — organized around a business domain or user journey. Each team owns a slice of the product end-to-end: frontend, backend, database, deployment. This is your primary team type. Most engineers belong to a stream-aligned team.

**Platform team** — builds and maintains the Internal Developer Platform. Stream-aligned teams are their customers. One platform team is sufficient up to ~150 engineers.

**Enabling team** — temporary teams that help stream-aligned teams adopt new capabilities. Example: a team of two senior engineers who spend 6 weeks helping each product team adopt distributed tracing, then disband. Use sparingly.

**Complicated-subsystem team** — owns a technically complex component that requires deep specialist knowledge. Example: the machine learning inference pipeline, the real-time data processing engine. Only create these when the complexity genuinely requires dedicated expertise.

### Sizing and spans

- **Team size**: 5-8 engineers per team. Smaller teams lack resilience (one person on vacation stalls the team). Larger teams have too much coordination overhead.
- **Manager span**: 5-8 direct reports per manager. Below 5, the manager role is not justified. Above 8, 1:1s and coaching quality degrades.
- **Director span**: 4-6 managers per director. Directors need enough time for strategy and cross-team coordination, not just manager 1:1s.

For 80 engineers growing to 120:

```
CTO
├── VP Engineering
│   ├── Director, Product Engineering (4 stream-aligned teams)
│   │   ├── EM: Team Growth (6 engineers)
│   │   ├── EM: Team Payments (7 engineers)
│   │   ├── EM: Team Engagement (6 engineers)
│   │   └── EM: Team Onboarding (6 engineers)
│   ├── Director, Platform & Infra (2 teams)
│   │   ├── EM: Platform (6 engineers)
│   │   └── EM: Infrastructure (5 engineers)
│   └── Director, Core Systems (3 stream-aligned teams)
│       ├── EM: Team Search (6 engineers)
│       ├── EM: Team Data Pipeline (7 engineers)
│       └── EM: Team API (6 engineers)
├── Staff Engineer (architecture, RFCs, cross-cutting concerns)
└── Principal Engineer (technical strategy, architecture council)
```

This gives you 9 teams averaging 6 engineers each (54 in the org chart, scaling to ~75 as teams grow and new teams form). Remaining headcount fills into existing teams or seeds new ones as the product expands.

## Hiring at scale

### The hiring bar

Define it explicitly. Write it down. Every interviewer should be able to articulate what "meets the bar" looks like for each role.

**The bar should be**: would this person make a team better, not just bigger? If the answer is "they're fine, we need the headcount," you are lowering the bar. That debt compounds faster than any technical debt.

### Structured interviews

Unstructured interviews are unreliable. Two interviewers can interview the same candidate and reach opposite conclusions. Structured interviews — where every candidate answers the same questions, evaluated against the same rubric — dramatically improve consistency.

For engineering roles, a typical structured loop:

1. **Technical screen** (45 min): live coding problem. Evaluates problem-solving, code quality, and communication.
2. **System design** (60 min): design a system at appropriate scale for the level. Evaluates architectural thinking and trade-off reasoning.
3. **Behavioral** (45 min): past experiences mapped to your team's values. "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a technical decision. What did you do?"
4. **Team fit** (45 min): a conversation with potential teammates. Not "do I want to get a beer with this person" — "can I collaborate with this person effectively?"

### Debrief and calibrate

After the loop, interviewers debrief synchronously. Each interviewer presents their evidence and recommendation before hearing others' opinions (to prevent anchoring). The hiring manager makes the final call, but must justify overriding a strong "no" from any interviewer.

## Engineering culture at scale

Culture is not the poster on the wall. Culture is what happens when nobody is watching. At 200 people, you cannot personally model every behavior. You define the values, hire people who embody them, and create systems that reinforce them.

### Define your engineering values

Pick 3-5 values. Make them specific enough to guide decisions. "We value excellence" is meaningless. "We ship when it's ready, not when it's due" is a value that changes behavior.

Example engineering values:

- **Own the outcome, not just the task.** If your feature ships but the user experience is broken, the job is not done.
- **Write it down.** Decisions, context, and reasoning belong in documents, not in Slack threads that scroll off-screen.
- **Default to transparency.** Share information broadly. Restricting information requires justification; sharing it does not.
- **Disagree and commit.** Debate vigorously before a decision. Execute wholeheartedly after it.

### Reinforcement mechanisms

Values without reinforcement are decorations. Reinforce them through:

- **Hiring**: screen for values in behavioral interviews. A brilliant engineer who hoards information violates "default to transparency" and will poison a team.
- **Promotions**: promote people who exemplify the values. If you promote someone who ships fast but leaves wreckage behind, you have told the org that wreckage is acceptable.
- **Recognition**: publicly recognize behavior that embodies values. "Team Search wrote a postmortem that prevented three future incidents. That's owning the outcome."
- **Accountability**: address values violations directly. A senior engineer who refuses to write documentation after repeated feedback is not a culture fit, regardless of their technical skill.

## Communication at scale

At 20 people, information flows naturally. At 200, it does not. You must design communication channels deliberately.

### Cadences

| Cadence   | Audience               | Purpose                               |
| --------- | ---------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Weekly    | Engineering all-hands  | Updates, wins, metrics, Q&A           |
| Biweekly  | Architecture council   | RFC reviews, standards decisions      |
| Monthly   | Engineering leadership | Roadmap progress, hiring, org health  |
| Quarterly | Full company           | Technology roadmap, major initiatives |

### Written communication

Meetings are synchronous and do not scale. Documents are asynchronous and scale infinitely. Establish a bias toward writing:

- Every decision has an ADR
- Every initiative has a one-page brief
- Every team has a weekly written update
- Every incident has a postmortem

This creates an institutional memory that survives employee turnover. When an engineer joins in month 18, they can read the history instead of reconstructing it from hallway conversations.

## Putting it together

Design your org as carefully as you would design a system. Define clear interfaces between teams (APIs, contracts, shared standards). Minimize coupling (teams should be able to ship independently). Maximize cohesion (everything a team needs to deliver value should be within the team).

Monitor organizational health the way you monitor system health. Attrition rates, engagement scores, DORA metrics, hiring pipeline velocity — these are your dashboards. When a metric moves in the wrong direction, investigate and intervene.

Your IDE has gathered dust. Your calendar is full of 1:1s, strategy sessions, and hiring debriefs. You spend your days in documents, not in code. And the engineering organization ships faster, more reliably, and more happily than it ever did when you were the one writing the code.

That is the job. That is the scale-up CTO.
