## Why this matters

The average CTO tenure at a public company is four to five years. Your departure — whether it is a promotion, a new company, retirement, or a termination — is not a question of if, but when. A CTO who has not developed successors leaves a vacuum that can destabilize the engineering organization for a year or more. A CTO who has built deep leadership leaves an organization that barely notices the transition.

This is counterintuitive. The human instinct is to make yourself indispensable — to be the person who knows everything, decides everything, and holds the organization together through sheer personal effort. That instinct is exactly wrong. The mark of a great leader is an organization that thrives without them. Succession planning is not about your exit — it is about building an organization that is robust to any single person leaving, including you.

## The bus factor problem

The "bus factor" is the number of people who could be hit by a bus (or, more optimistically, win the lottery and quit) before the organization cannot function. If your technology organization's bus factor is one — and that one person is you — you have not built a leadership team. You have built a dependency.

Audit your own bus factor honestly:

- How many decisions require your personal approval?
- How many meetings cannot happen without you?
- How many relationships (with the board, with key vendors, with the CEO) exist only through you?
- If you disappeared for three months, what would break?

The answers reveal where you are a bottleneck. Each bottleneck is a succession risk.

## Building leadership depth

Succession planning is not about picking your replacement. It is about building a leadership pipeline — multiple people at multiple levels who are ready to step up when the time comes.

### The three layers

**Layer 1: Your direct reports (VPs of Engineering).** At least two of your VPs should be capable of stepping into the CTO role within 12 months. This does not mean they are identical to you — it means they have the strategic thinking, stakeholder management, and technical credibility to lead the function.

**Layer 2: Directors and senior directors.** These are the people who will become your VPs' replacements when your VPs step up. They should be developing the same skills at a smaller scale — setting strategy for their domains, managing up to the VP, and building their own teams.

**Layer 3: Senior engineers and engineering managers.** The future directors. They may be five to seven years away from the CTO chair, but their development starts now. Identify the ones with both technical excellence and leadership potential, and give them deliberate growth opportunities.

### Development strategies

**Rotation.** Move leaders across domains. A VP who has only ever led backend teams should spend a year leading a platform or infrastructure team. Breadth of experience is essential for the CTO role, and rotation builds it.

**Stretch assignments.** Give leaders responsibilities slightly beyond their current level. Ask a director to present the quarterly technology review to the board. Ask a VP to lead a cross-organizational initiative. These assignments are safe enough to fail (you are there as a backstop) and challenging enough to grow.

**Shadow program.** For your top succession candidates, create explicit shadowing: they attend your board presentations, join your 1:1 with the CEO, and sit in on your budget negotiations. Not as passive observers — as participants who prepare their own perspectives and debrief with you afterward.

**External exposure.** Send your leaders to CTO peer groups, industry conferences, and executive education programs. The transition from VP to CTO requires skills that are hard to develop inside one company — investor communication, industry-level strategy, board dynamics.

## The succession document

Every CTO should maintain a confidential succession document, updated annually, that covers:

1. **Current state of the leadership team.** Strengths, development areas, and readiness assessment for each VP.
2. **Emergency succession.** If you are unavailable tomorrow, who acts as interim CTO? This person must have the trust of the CEO, the board, and the engineering organization. Name them explicitly and ensure they know they are named.
3. **Planned succession (12-month horizon).** If you announced your departure today with a 6-month transition, who would you recommend? What development do they need in those 6 months?
4. **Long-term pipeline (3-year horizon).** Who are the directors and senior managers who could be CTO-ready in three years? What experiences do they need?
5. **Gaps.** Where is the leadership bench thin? What roles would you need to hire externally if a key leader departed?

Share a version of this document with your CHRO (Chief Human Resources Officer) and your CEO. They need to know the state of the leadership pipeline. The details of individual assessments may stay confidential, but the overall bench strength should be transparent.

## Developing your replacement is not threatening

The most common objection to succession planning is unstated: "If I develop my replacement, what happens to me?" The fear is that making yourself dispensable makes yourself disposable.

The opposite is true. A CTO who has built a deep leadership bench is more valuable, not less. The board sees an organization that is resilient. The CEO sees a leader who thinks beyond their own tenure. Other companies see a CTO who can build teams, not just manage them. The best path to your next opportunity — whether it is a bigger CTO role, a CEO role, a board seat, or a venture firm — is a track record of building leaders.

The CTOs who cling to indispensability eventually face a different problem: they cannot leave even when they want to, because the organization would fall apart. That is not job security. It is a trap.

## Knowledge transfer

Beyond developing people, succession planning requires capturing and transferring institutional knowledge:

**Decision logs.** The RFC and ADR archives are succession tools. They contain the reasoning behind decisions that a new CTO would otherwise have to rediscover.

**Relationship maps.** The CTO maintains relationships with dozens of external stakeholders: board members, key vendors, industry peers, regulators. Document these relationships — who they are, what they care about, and the history of the relationship. A successor who has to rebuild these from scratch loses months.

**Operating rhythms.** Document the recurring meetings, reports, and rituals that keep the technology organization running. Monthly board deck preparation, quarterly planning, annual budgeting, weekly leadership team meeting — a successor who inherits these rhythms can focus on strategy instead of logistics.

**Unwritten rules.** Every organization has them. The board member who always asks about security. The business unit leader who needs extra communication before changes. The CEO's communication preferences. Write them down.

Bill Campbell, the legendary Silicon Valley coach, had a rule: "Your title makes you a manager. Your people make you a leader." The CTOs who are remembered — not for the systems they built, but for the leaders they developed — understood that their most important product was not software. It was the people who would build software long after they were gone.

## What you learned

Succession planning is a continuous practice, not an exit strategy. The CTO builds leadership depth across three layers — VPs, directors, and senior managers — through rotation, stretch assignments, shadow programs, and external exposure. A confidential succession document names emergency, planned, and long-term successors. Knowledge transfer through decision logs, relationship maps, and documented operating rhythms ensures continuity. The best CTOs make themselves dispensable — and that is precisely what makes them irreplaceable.
