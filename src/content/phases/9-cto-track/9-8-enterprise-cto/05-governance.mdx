## Why this matters

At 50 engineers, alignment happens in the hallway. At 500, it does not happen at all unless you build systems for it. Technology governance is the set of structures, processes, and norms that keep a large engineering organization making consistent, high-quality decisions without requiring every decision to flow through the CTO's office.

The word "governance" makes engineers flinch. They hear bureaucracy — committees, approval gates, and weeks of delay before they can pick a library. Bad governance is exactly that. Good governance is invisible to engineers making routine decisions and only surfaces for the decisions that genuinely need organizational alignment. The CTO's job is to build the latter.

## Architecture Review Board

The Architecture Review Board (ARB) is a group of senior engineers and architects who review significant technical decisions before they are finalized. Not every decision — only the ones that cross team boundaries, introduce new technologies, or create long-lived commitments.

### What triggers a review

Define clear criteria so teams know when to engage the ARB and when to proceed independently:

- **New database engine or data store** — because data layer decisions affect every team downstream
- **New programming language or runtime** — because it fragments the hiring pipeline and tooling investment
- **Cross-team API contracts** — because breaking changes cascade
- **Infrastructure that costs more than $X per month** — because cloud bills are everyone's problem
- **Architectural patterns that contradict existing standards** — not to block, but to ensure the deviation is intentional

### What does NOT trigger a review

- Choice of npm package within an existing category
- Internal refactoring that does not change external contracts
- Feature implementation within established patterns
- UI component library choices (within approved design system)

### How the board operates

**Composition:** 5-7 members. Rotating membership with staggered terms (12-18 months) so institutional knowledge is preserved while fresh perspectives join. Include at least one member from infrastructure, one from a product team, and one from security.

**Cadence:** Weekly 60-minute sessions. Teams submit proposals in advance using a standard template. If the queue is empty, cancel the meeting.

**Output:** Three possible outcomes: approved, approved with conditions, or deferred for more information. Never "rejected" — a deferred proposal comes back with answers to the board's questions. The goal is to improve decisions, not to block them.

**SLA:** No proposal waits more than two weeks for a decision. If the board cannot decide in two sessions, it escalates to the CTO for a tiebreak.

### The proposal template

```markdown
# Architecture Decision Proposal

## Title

## Context

What problem are we solving? What constraints exist?

## Decision

What are we proposing?

## Alternatives Considered

What else did we evaluate? Why did we reject those options?

## Consequences

What are the tradeoffs? What do we gain and what do we lose?

## Reversibility

How hard is it to undo this decision if it turns out to be wrong?

## Affected Teams

Who needs to know about or adapt to this decision?
```

This format — borrowed from Architecture Decision Records — forces the proposing team to think through their decision rigorously before presenting it. Half the value of the ARB is the preparation it requires.

## Technology Radar

The Technology Radar is a living document that classifies every technology the organization uses (or might use) into four categories:

**Adopt:** Proven, supported, recommended for new projects. These are the default choices. An engineer starting a new service should reach for Adopt technologies first.

**Trial:** Promising technologies that one or two teams are actively testing in production. Engineers can use Trial technologies with ARB awareness but not approval.

**Assess:** Technologies worth watching but not yet proven in your context. Engineers should experiment with Assess technologies in hackathons and side projects, not production services.

**Hold:** Technologies you are actively moving away from. No new projects should use Hold technologies. Existing usage is migrated on a prioritized schedule.

### Maintaining the radar

Update the radar quarterly. The process:

1. Engineering leads nominate technologies for movement (e.g., "move X from Trial to Adopt" or "move Y from Adopt to Hold")
2. The ARB reviews nominations with supporting evidence
3. The updated radar is published to the entire engineering organization
4. The CTO writes a brief commentary explaining significant changes

The radar is a communication tool, not an enforcement tool. It tells engineers "here is what we recommend and why" — it does not prevent them from making different choices. But if a team wants to use a Hold technology, they know they will need to explain why at the ARB.

### Example radar entries

```
ADOPT:   TypeScript, PostgreSQL, Kubernetes, React, Terraform
TRIAL:   Deno, CockroachDB, WebAssembly edge functions
ASSESS:  AI code assistants, WASM component model, SQLite for serverless
HOLD:    AngularJS, MongoDB (for relational data), Jenkins, Heroku
```

## Engineering standards

Standards codify the decisions that should not be re-debated. They are the answers to questions that every team asks and that should have the same answer everywhere:

- How do we structure a service repository?
- How do we handle authentication between services?
- What logging format do we use?
- How do we version APIs?
- What does a production-ready service include (health checks, metrics, alerts)?

### The golden path

The "golden path" is the paved road — a set of templates, tooling, and documentation that makes it easy to do the right thing. Instead of publishing a 30-page standards document and hoping engineers read it, you build tools that embed the standards:

- **Service template:** `create-service` command that scaffolds a new service with the correct structure, linting configuration, CI pipeline, Dockerfile, and monitoring setup
- **Shared libraries:** Internal packages for logging, authentication, configuration, and error handling that encode standards in code
- **CI checks:** Automated enforcement of naming conventions, dependency policies, and security requirements

The golden path philosophy: make the right thing the easy thing. If following the standard is harder than ignoring it, the standard will be ignored.

## The RFC process

For decisions too large for a single ARB session — platform migrations, major architecture shifts, new organizational standards — use a Request for Comments (RFC) process:

1. **Author writes the RFC** using a standard template (similar to the ARB proposal but longer and more detailed)
2. **Review period (2 weeks):** The RFC is published to the engineering organization. Anyone can comment. The author is expected to respond to every substantive comment.
3. **Decision meeting:** The author presents the RFC to stakeholders. Open questions are resolved. The decision is made.
4. **Publication:** The final RFC is archived with its decision and rationale. Future engineers can read it to understand why things are the way they are.

RFCs create institutional memory. Three years from now, when someone asks "why do we use X instead of Y?", the RFC is the answer. Without it, the reasoning is lost when the people who made the decision leave the company.

## Balancing governance and autonomy

The tension in all governance is between consistency and speed. Too much governance and teams cannot move. Too little and the organization fragments into incompatible fiefdoms.

The principle: **govern the interfaces, free the internals.** How services communicate with each other — API contracts, data formats, authentication — must be standardized. How a team builds their service internally — code structure, testing approach, internal libraries — is their business.

This mirrors the microservices principle at an organizational level. Teams are autonomous within their boundaries. The boundaries themselves are governed.

### Signs governance is too heavy

- Teams spend more than 10% of their time on compliance activities
- The ARB has a backlog longer than two weeks
- Engineers routinely work around governance processes rather than through them
- New service creation takes more than a day of setup work

### Signs governance is too light

- Multiple teams independently build the same capability
- Production incidents are caused by incompatible assumptions between services
- New engineers cannot figure out the "right way" to do common tasks
- Technology choices proliferate without rationale

## What you learned

Technology governance keeps large engineering organizations aligned without crushing autonomy. The Architecture Review Board reviews decisions that cross team boundaries, using a standard proposal template and a strict SLA. The Technology Radar communicates recommended, experimental, and deprecated technologies. Engineering standards are embedded in golden path tooling that makes the right thing the easy thing. And the RFC process creates institutional memory for the decisions that shape the organization for years. The governing principle: standardize the interfaces, free the internals.
