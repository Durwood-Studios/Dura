## Why this matters

A technology strategy is not a list of projects. It is a theory about how technology will create business value over the next three to five years — and a plan for allocating resources to test that theory. Without a strategy, you are just doing whatever is loudest. With a bad strategy, you burn millions chasing trends. With a good strategy, every team in the organization understands why they are building what they are building, and the company compounds its technology investments instead of scattering them.

Most CTOs have a strategy in their head. Few write it down. Fewer still pressure-test it against the board's expectations, the CFO's budget constraints, and the reality of what the engineering organization can actually deliver. This lesson is about the discipline of making your strategy explicit, defensible, and adaptable.

## The horizon framework

Long-range planning does not mean predicting the future. It means structuring your investments across time horizons so that you are simultaneously maintaining today's systems, improving near-term capabilities, and placing bets on the future.

### Horizon 1: Sustain and optimize (0-12 months)

This is the core business. The platforms and products that generate revenue today. Horizon 1 work is not glamorous — it is reliability improvements, performance optimization, security hardening, and incremental feature delivery. It is also where most of your engineering capacity goes: typically 60-70% of total investment.

The mistake CTOs make with Horizon 1 is neglect. It is tempting to spend all your attention on the exciting future-state architecture while the existing systems quietly accumulate debt. But Horizon 1 is your credibility. If production goes down, nobody cares about your three-year vision.

### Horizon 2: Extend and build (12-24 months)

New capabilities that extend the existing business. A new platform, a new product line built on existing infrastructure, a major architectural evolution. Horizon 2 work typically consumes 20-30% of investment.

Horizon 2 is where strategy meets execution. These are the big bets that require cross-team coordination, new hires, and sustained focus. The CTO's job is to protect Horizon 2 work from being cannibalized by Horizon 1 urgencies. Every week, someone will argue that a Horizon 2 engineer should be pulled onto a production incident or a high-priority customer request. Sometimes they are right. Usually they are not.

### Horizon 3: Explore and experiment (24-36+ months)

Emerging technologies, proof-of-concepts, and strategic experiments. Horizon 3 work consumes 5-10% of investment. It is explicitly uncertain — most Horizon 3 bets will not pan out. The ones that do become Horizon 2 projects next year.

## Evaluating emerging technology

Every year brings a wave of technologies that promise to transform your industry. Your job is not to adopt them all or ignore them all — it is to evaluate them systematically so your organization makes informed bets rather than emotional ones.

### The evaluation framework

For any emerging technology, answer five questions before investing:

**1. What problem does this solve that we actually have?** Not "what could this do in theory" — what specific, measurable problem in our business does this address? If you cannot name the problem, you are chasing novelty.

**2. What is the maturity curve?** Is this technology in research, early adoption, mainstream, or commodity? The right time to invest depends on your risk tolerance and competitive position. First movers pay the cost of immature tooling. Fast followers benefit from others' mistakes.

**3. What is the switching cost?** If you adopt this and it fails, how expensive is the reversal? A new CI tool has low switching cost. A new database engine has high switching cost. Match your commitment level to the reversibility of the decision.

**4. What is the talent market?** Can you hire people who know this technology? Can you train your existing team? A brilliant technology that nobody on your team can operate is a liability, not an asset.

**5. What does the second-order effect look like?** If this technology succeeds, what changes downstream? If you adopt a real-time data platform, your product teams will want to build real-time features. Are you ready for that demand? Technologies are never isolated — they create appetite.

### The pilot protocol

When a technology passes evaluation, run a time-boxed pilot before committing:

- **Scope:** One team, one use case, 6-8 weeks
- **Success criteria:** Defined before the pilot starts, not after
- **Kill criteria:** What would cause you to stop early?
- **Documentation:** The team records what worked, what did not, and what surprised them
- **Decision gate:** At the end, the pilot team recommends adopt, defer, or reject — and the CTO makes the call

## Writing the strategy document

A three-year technology strategy document should be 10-15 pages. More than that and nobody reads it. Less than that and it lacks the specificity to be actionable.

### Structure

1. **Business context.** Where is the company going? What are the CEO's top three priorities? What market shifts are we responding to? The technology strategy exists to serve the business strategy — start there.

2. **Current state assessment.** Honest evaluation of where technology stands today. Strengths, weaknesses, technical debt, team capabilities. Do not sugarcoat this section. If the board discovers problems you did not disclose, your credibility evaporates.

3. **Target state vision.** What does the technology landscape look like in three years? Describe the architecture, the platforms, the capabilities, and the team structure you are building toward. Be specific enough to be wrong — "cloud-native microservices architecture" is a buzzword. "All new services deployed as containers on Kubernetes with a service mesh handling cross-cutting concerns" is a strategy.

4. **Horizon roadmap.** Map initiatives to Horizon 1, 2, and 3 with rough timelines and investment levels. This is not a project plan — it is a resource allocation framework.

5. **Investment requirements.** How much money and how many people. The CFO will read this section first. Be precise about what you need and what the expected return is.

6. **Risks and dependencies.** What could go wrong? What do you need from other parts of the business? Name the assumptions that, if wrong, would change the strategy.

7. **Governance.** How you will track progress, make course corrections, and communicate to stakeholders. A strategy without governance is a wish.

## Strategic pivots

No three-year strategy survives three years unchanged. The discipline is not in sticking to the plan — it is in knowing when to pivot and when to stay the course.

Signs a strategic pivot is needed:

- A fundamental business assumption has changed (acquisition, market shift, regulatory change)
- A Horizon 3 bet has matured faster than expected and demands accelerated investment
- The talent market has shifted in a way that makes your planned approach impractical
- A competitor has moved first, changing your risk calculus

Signs you should stay the course despite pressure:

- Results are slower than hoped but the thesis is intact
- A shiny new technology appears but does not solve a problem you have
- A vocal minority of engineers wants to chase something different
- One quarter of poor results in a multi-year investment

The hardest skill in long-range strategy is distinguishing signal from noise. Boards, competitors, vendors, and your own team will all generate noise. Your job is to absorb it, filter it, and change direction only when the evidence demands it.

## What you learned

A three-year technology strategy is a resource allocation framework built on a theory of value creation. It uses horizon planning to balance maintenance, growth, and exploration. Emerging technologies are evaluated against specific business problems, not hype cycles. And the strategy document itself is a communication tool — it aligns stakeholders around a shared vision while providing the governance structure to adapt when reality diverges from the plan.
