## Why this matters

Your tech stack is a decision you make once and live with for years. The technologies you choose on day one determine who you can hire, how fast you can ship, what breaks at 3 AM, and how much it costs to change course. Most startups that fail for "technical reasons" did not fail because they picked the wrong language. They failed because they picked too many interesting technologies at once and spent their energy fighting tools instead of shipping product.

## The boring technology thesis

Dan McKinley's "Choose Boring Technology" essay is one of the most important pieces of engineering writing for startup CTOs. The core argument: every company gets roughly three innovation tokens. Spend them on technology choices that genuinely differentiate your product. Spend them on nothing else.

Boring technology is technology that is well-understood, well-documented, has a large hiring pool, and has known failure modes. PostgreSQL is boring. React is boring. Node.js is boring. "Boring" is the highest compliment you can give a piece of infrastructure.

When you choose boring technology:

- **Stack Overflow has your error message**. Thousands of engineers have hit the same bug before you.
- **You can hire for it**. Posting a job for "Node.js developer" produces hundreds of candidates. Posting for "Gleam developer" produces five.
- **Failure modes are documented**. You know what happens when Postgres runs out of connections. You do not know what happens when the new distributed database you picked loses a quorum node.
- **Libraries exist**. Authentication, payments, email — someone has already built the integration you need.

```typescript
// Innovation token budget for a typical startup
interface InnovationBudget {
  readonly totalTokens: 3;
  // Spend tokens ONLY on what differentiates you
  tokens: [
    "Our ML-powered recommendation engine", // Core product differentiator
    "Custom real-time collaboration protocol", // Key feature competitors lack
    "???", // Save one for emergencies
  ];
  // Everything else: BORING
  boring: {
    language: "TypeScript"; // Massive hiring pool
    framework: "Next.js"; // Proven, documented, supported
    database: "PostgreSQL"; // 30+ years of battle-testing
    hosting: "Vercel" | "Railway"; // Managed, scales automatically
    auth: "Supabase Auth"; // Do not build your own auth
    payments: "Stripe"; // Do not build your own payments
  };
}
```

## How stack decisions compound

A technology choice is never just that choice. It is a cascade:

1. You pick **TypeScript**. Now every hire must know TypeScript or learn it. Every library must have type definitions. Your build pipeline needs a compilation step. Your editor tooling is configured for TS.

2. You pick **Next.js**. Now your routing is file-based. Your deployment target is optimized for Vercel. Your API routes live alongside your frontend. Your team learns React Server Components.

3. You pick **Supabase**. Now your auth uses JWTs from Supabase. Your database is Postgres with RLS policies. Your real-time features use Supabase channels. Your migrations use the Supabase CLI.

Each decision narrows the space of future decisions. This is not a problem — it is the point. Narrowing your decision space means fewer debates, faster onboarding, and more time shipping. The danger is when you narrow into a dead end: a framework that stops being maintained, a database that cannot handle your access pattern, a hosting provider that triples their pricing.

## The evaluation framework

When choosing a technology, run it through five questions:

| Question                                      | Why it matters                                               |
| --------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **How many engineers know this?**             | Hiring pool and onboarding speed                             |
| **What happens when things break?**           | Known failure modes vs. unknown territory                    |
| **Who maintains it?**                         | Single maintainer OSS vs. backed by a company vs. foundation |
| **What is the migration path out?**           | Vendor lock-in cost if you need to leave                     |
| **Does it solve a problem we actually have?** | Solving future problems creates present complexity           |

The last question is the one most founders get wrong. They pick technologies for the company they want to be in three years instead of the company they are today. You do not need a message queue when you have twelve users. You do not need a CDN when your assets are three images. You do not need Redis when your database queries take 4ms.

## Real-world stack comparisons

### The startup that moved fast

```
Frontend: Next.js + Tailwind
Backend:  Next.js API routes → Supabase
Database: Supabase (Postgres)
Auth:     Supabase Auth
Hosting:  Vercel
CI/CD:    GitHub Actions
Monitoring: Sentry + Vercel Analytics

Total services to manage: 3 (Vercel, Supabase, GitHub)
Time to first deploy: 2 hours
Time to hire first engineer: Found someone in 2 weeks
```

### The startup that moved slow

```
Frontend: SvelteKit + custom design system
Backend:  Rust (Actix-web) → custom GraphQL layer
Database: CockroachDB (distributed SQL)
Auth:     Custom JWT implementation
Hosting:  AWS ECS with Terraform
CI/CD:    Self-hosted Drone CI
Monitoring: Grafana + Prometheus + Loki stack

Total services to manage: 11
Time to first deploy: 3 weeks
Time to hire first engineer: Searched for 4 months
```

Both stacks are technically sound. Only one is appropriate for a two-person startup that needs to find product-market fit before the runway ends.

## When to break the boring technology rule

Boring technology is the default, not the law. Break it when:

- **The boring option genuinely cannot do what you need**. If you are building a real-time multiplayer game, you might need a technology specifically designed for that. But verify the boring option truly fails before reaching for the exotic one.
- **The exotic technology IS your product**. If you are building a database company, your database is allowed to be novel. That is your innovation token.
- **You have deep expertise in the non-boring option**. If your founding team has ten years of Elixir experience, Elixir is boring to you. The hiring pool argument weakens when you already have the team.

## What you learned

- The boring technology thesis: spend innovation tokens only on what differentiates your product; use proven, well-documented tools for everything else.
- Stack decisions compound — each choice narrows future choices, affects hiring, and determines what breaks at 3 AM.
- Evaluate technology through five lenses: hiring pool, failure modes, maintenance, migration cost, and whether it solves a problem you actually have today.
- The most common startup mistake is picking technology for the company you want to be instead of the company you are.
