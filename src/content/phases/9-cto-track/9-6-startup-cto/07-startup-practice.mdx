## Why this matters

Everything in this module has been theory. Theory is worthless without practice. This lesson asks you to synthesize everything — founding engineer survival, stack selection, hiring, MVP scope, investor pitching, and sustainable velocity — into a single cohesive plan. This is the exercise that separates people who understand startup engineering from people who can do it.

## The scenario

You are the founding CTO of **Relay**, a B2B SaaS platform that helps logistics companies optimize last-mile delivery routes. Here are the facts:

- **Funding**: $1.2M pre-seed. Monthly burn rate is $40K (salaries + infrastructure + office).
- **Runway**: 30 months at current burn, but you need to raise Series A in 18 months, which means demonstrable traction by month 15.
- **Team today**: You (CTO) and the CEO (non-technical, strong in sales and logistics domain expertise).
- **Product status**: The CEO has been selling the concept using a spreadsheet and Google Maps. Three logistics companies have signed letters of intent. They expect a working product in 4 months.
- **Technical requirements**: Route optimization engine, driver mobile app, dispatcher web dashboard, real-time GPS tracking, integration with existing fleet management systems.
- **Competitive landscape**: Two funded competitors exist. One is enterprise-only (slow, expensive). The other is a lightweight tool that does not handle real-time tracking.

Your job: design the first six months.

## What you learned

- Planning six months of startup engineering requires synthesizing stack selection, MVP scoping, hiring sequencing, velocity management, and investor preparation into a single coherent strategy.
- Every decision is constrained by runway, team size, and customer commitments. The plan must account for all three simultaneously.
- The practice of writing a concrete plan — with specific tasks, dates, and names — reveals gaps that abstract knowledge does not.
- No plan survives contact with reality, but the exercise of planning builds the judgment you need when reality forces you to adapt.
