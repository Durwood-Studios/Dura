## Why this matters

There is no engineering team on day one. There is you. You write the code, deploy the infrastructure, fix the 2 AM outage, answer the security questionnaire from the first enterprise prospect, and wire the payment integration before the demo on Thursday. The founding engineer role is not a job title. It is a survival mode.

Understanding what this role actually demands — before you accept it — determines whether you build a foundation that scales or a house of cards that collapses at ten customers.

## What the founding engineer actually does

Your calendar does not distinguish between "CTO work" and "engineer work" because both are you. A typical week in the first six months looks like this:

- **Monday**: Ship a feature the CEO promised in a sales call last Friday.
- **Tuesday**: Debug a production issue that affects the only paying customer. Deploy the fix. Write a postmortem nobody reads.
- **Wednesday**: Evaluate three SaaS tools for customer support. Pick one. Integrate it.
- **Thursday**: Pair with the designer (who is also the CEO's college roommate doing this part-time) to build the onboarding flow.
- **Friday**: Refactor the authentication system because you hard-coded things on Monday that now break the enterprise trial.

This is not dysfunction. This is the founding engineer reality. The ratio is roughly 80% writing code and 20% firefighting everything else — security, DevOps, vendor evaluation, technical sales support, and explaining to the CEO why "just adding AI" is not a weekend project.

## The bus factor problem

On day one, your bus factor is one. If you get sick, the entire engineering operation stops. This is not acceptable for long, but it is unavoidable at first. Your job is to minimize the blast radius:

- **Document your infrastructure decisions** as you make them. Not polished docs — a running log in a `DECISIONS.md` file that says what you chose, why, and what the alternatives were. Future engineers will thank you.
- **Use managed services ruthlessly**. This is not the time to run your own database cluster. Managed Postgres, managed Redis, managed CI/CD. Every piece of infrastructure you self-host is a piece of infrastructure only you can fix at 2 AM.
- **Automate deployments from day one**. If deploying requires a sequence of manual steps that only you know, you have created a single point of failure that compounds every day.

```markdown
# DECISIONS.md — Engineering Decision Log

## 2024-01-15: Database choice

**Decision**: Supabase (managed Postgres + auth + realtime)
**Why**: Eliminates separate auth service, provides row-level security,
free tier covers MVP traffic. One fewer vendor to manage.
**Alternatives considered**: PlanetScale (MySQL, no built-in auth),
Firebase (vendor lock-in concerns, NoSQL)
**Revisit when**: We exceed 100k monthly active users or need
multi-region writes.
```

## The compounding effect of early decisions

Every technical decision you make in the first three months will be ten times harder to change in month twelve and a hundred times harder in month thirty-six. This is the compounding problem of founding engineering.

The database you pick becomes the database the team learns, the ORM wraps, the migration scripts target, and the monitoring dashboards graph. Switching later means rewriting queries, retraining engineers, and migrating production data under load. The same applies to your hosting provider, your authentication system, your API design patterns, and your deployment pipeline.

This does not mean you should spend weeks evaluating options. It means you should pick boring, proven technology and move fast. The cost of a wrong decision made quickly is almost always lower than the cost of a correct decision made slowly — because in a startup, the thing that kills you is not picking the wrong database. It is not shipping.

## What you should NOT do

- **Do not build a microservices architecture**. You are one person. A monolith deployed as a single unit is the correct architecture until you have a team large enough to own separate services. Every startup that starts with microservices regrets it.
- **Do not optimize for scale you do not have**. Your first ten customers do not need Kubernetes. They need features that work.
- **Do not skip monitoring**. You cannot fix what you cannot see. Set up error tracking (Sentry), uptime monitoring (Betteruptime), and basic logging on day one. This takes two hours and saves you hundreds.
- **Do not write code without version control**. This sounds obvious, but the pressure to "just push something live" is real. Every change goes through git. No exceptions.

## The identity shift

The hardest part of being a founding engineer is not technical. It is the identity shift from "engineer who writes great code" to "engineer who ships what the business needs." You will write code you are not proud of. You will take shortcuts you would never take at a larger company. You will ship features that are 70% polished because 70% today beats 100% next month.

This is not lowering your standards. It is choosing the right standard for the current context. The standard at a startup is not "is this elegant?" It is "does this work, is it safe, and can I change it later?" If the answer to all three is yes, ship it.

## What you learned

- The founding engineer IS the engineering department — there is no separation between coding, DevOps, security, and technical sales support.
- A bus factor of one is unavoidable at first but must be mitigated through documentation, managed services, and deployment automation.
- Early technical decisions compound — pick boring technology, decide quickly, and move on.
- The correct quality standard for a startup is "does it work, is it safe, and can I change it later?" — not "is it elegant?"
