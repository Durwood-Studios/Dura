## Why this matters

Strategy is not an abstraction — it is a document you write, defend, and execute against. This lesson is your opportunity to synthesize roadmapping, prioritization, metrics, stakeholder communication, and product thinking into a single coherent artifact. The scenario is fictional. The skills are not.

## The scenario

You are the CTO of **Stackline**, a B2B SaaS platform that helps DevOps teams monitor and manage their cloud infrastructure. The company is 18 months old, has raised a Series A, and is post-product-market-fit but pre-scale.

**Current state:**

```
Team: 14 engineers (3 teams of 4 + 2 platform engineers)
ARR: $2.4M, growing 15% QoQ
Customers: 85 paying accounts, average contract $28K/year
Churn: 8% annually (acceptable but not great)
NPS: 42 (good, not excellent)
DORA: Deploying 3x/week, lead time 4 days, failure rate 22%, MTTR 6 hours
Tech debt: CI pipeline takes 18 minutes; monitoring service is a monolith
  that three teams all need to modify; no feature flag system
```

**Inputs from stakeholders:**

- **CEO:** "We need to close enterprise deals. Three prospects in the pipeline need SSO, audit logging, and SOC 2 compliance. Combined deal value: $450K ARR."
- **VP Sales:** "Our trial-to-paid conversion is 12%. Industry benchmark is 25%. The onboarding experience is confusing — prospects give up before seeing value."
- **VP Product:** "Customer interviews reveal that alerting is our weakest feature. Users set up monitors but the alert routing and escalation is primitive. Two customers churned citing this specifically."
- **Head of Support:** "Ticket volume is up 40% QoQ. Most tickets are about the same three configuration issues. We need better documentation or a configuration wizard."
- **Platform engineers:** "The monitoring monolith is killing us. Every feature change requires coordinating across all three teams. We estimate it costs 30% of our velocity."

**Your constraints:**

- 14 engineers × 12 weeks = 168 engineering-weeks of raw capacity
- After meetings, on-call, sick days, holidays: ~120 effective engineering-weeks
- You committed to 20% tech debt allocation: 24 engineering-weeks for technical health
- Remaining feature capacity: 96 engineering-weeks

## Exercise 1: Define your themes

Using the inputs above, define 3-4 themes for Q3. For each theme, write:

1. A theme name (2-4 words)
2. The business rationale (1-2 sentences)
3. The expected outcome (measurable)
4. Rough capacity estimate

Think before you read the example below. There is no single correct answer — the goal is a defensible allocation that you could present to the CEO.

---

**Example themes (one possible answer):**

```
Theme 1: Enterprise Readiness (40 engineering-weeks)
  Rationale: $450K in pipeline requires SSO, audit logs, and SOC 2
  groundwork. This is 19% of current ARR in a single quarter.
  Outcome: Close at least 2 of 3 enterprise prospects by end of Q3.
  Work: SSO/SAML integration, audit logging, SOC 2 evidence collection.

Theme 2: Activation & Onboarding (32 engineering-weeks)
  Rationale: Trial-to-paid at 12% vs. 25% benchmark means we are
  losing 50%+ of potential revenue at the top of the funnel.
  Outcome: Trial-to-paid conversion reaches 18% by end of Q3.
  Work: Configuration wizard, guided onboarding flow, quick-start
  templates for common cloud providers.

Theme 3: Alert Quality (24 engineering-weeks)
  Rationale: Two churned customers cited alerting. Alerting NPS
  subscore is 28 vs. 42 overall. This is the biggest gap.
  Outcome: Alerting NPS reaches 38. Zero churn citing alerting in Q3.
  Work: Alert routing rules, escalation policies, PagerDuty/Opsgenie
  integration.

Theme 4: Technical Health (24 engineering-weeks — standing 20%)
  Rationale: Monolith coordination tax is costing 30% velocity.
  CI at 18 minutes burns 35 engineering-hours per week.
  Outcome: Monolith split into 2 services. CI under 8 minutes.
  Work: Extract alerting service from monolith. Parallelize CI.
  Add feature flag system.
```

**What was cut (and why):**

- Documentation improvements (VP Support's request): Addressed partially by the configuration wizard in Theme 2. A dedicated documentation sprint is deferred to Q4.
- Full alerting overhaul: Scoped to routing and escalation only. Advanced analytics deferred.

Notice the "what was cut" section. This is where you practice saying no — explicitly and with rationale.

## Exercise 2: Prioritize within a theme

Take Theme 2 (Activation & Onboarding) and apply RICE scoring to its three components:

```
Configuration Wizard
  Reach: 500 trial users/quarter
  Impact: 3 (massive — directly addresses #1 support ticket)
  Confidence: 80%
  Effort: 3 person-months
  RICE = (500 × 3 × 0.8) / 3 = 400

Guided Onboarding Flow
  Reach: 500 trial users/quarter
  Impact: 2 (high — improves first-session experience)
  Confidence: 60%
  Effort: 2 person-months
  RICE = (500 × 2 × 0.6) / 2 = 300

Quick-Start Templates
  Reach: 300 trial users/quarter (not all use supported providers)
  Impact: 1 (medium — saves setup time but not critical path)
  Confidence: 90%
  Effort: 1 person-month
  RICE = (300 × 1 × 0.9) / 1 = 270
```

The RICE scores suggest building the configuration wizard first, then the onboarding flow, then templates if capacity allows. Apply MoSCoW as a sanity check:

```
Must Have:  Configuration wizard (highest RICE, fixes top support issue)
Should Have: Guided onboarding flow (strong RICE, less certain impact)
Could Have:  Quick-start templates (good efficiency but lower reach)
```

## Exercise 3: Define metrics

For each theme, define one leading and one lagging indicator:

```
Theme 1: Enterprise Readiness
  Leading: Feature completion rate against enterprise checklist (weekly)
  Lagging: Enterprise deals closed (end of quarter)

Theme 2: Activation & Onboarding
  Leading: Day-1 activation rate (% completing first monitor setup)
  Lagging: Trial-to-paid conversion rate

Theme 3: Alert Quality
  Leading: Alert configuration completion rate (% of users with
    at least one routing rule)
  Lagging: Alerting NPS subscore

Theme 4: Technical Health
  Leading: CI build duration (measured daily)
  Lagging: Cross-team coordination incidents per sprint
```

## Exercise 4: Write the executive summary

Draft the one-page summary you would share with the CEO and board. Practice translating technical work into business language.

---

**Example:**

> **Q3 2026 Engineering Roadmap — Stackline**
>
> **Investment thesis:** Q3 focuses on two revenue accelerators (enterprise readiness and trial conversion) and one retention play (alert quality), supported by a standing investment in platform velocity.
>
> **Enterprise Readiness (33% of capacity).** Three enterprise prospects representing $450K ARR require SSO, audit logging, and SOC 2 evidence. We will deliver all three capabilities by Week 10, giving Sales four weeks to close before quarter end. Target: close 2 of 3 deals.
>
> **Activation & Onboarding (27% of capacity).** Our 12% trial-to-paid conversion is half the industry benchmark. A configuration wizard and guided onboarding flow will reduce time-to-first-value from 45 minutes to under 10. Target: reach 18% conversion by quarter end.
>
> **Alert Quality (20% of capacity).** Alerting is our lowest-rated feature and was cited in two churn events. We will add routing rules and escalation policies to close the gap with competitors. Target: alerting NPS from 28 to 38.
>
> **Technical Health (20% of capacity).** The monitoring monolith is costing 30% engineering velocity across all three teams. We will extract the alerting service and cut CI time from 18 to 8 minutes, recovering approximately 35 engineering-hours per week for the rest of the year.
>
> **What we are not doing this quarter:** Full documentation overhaul (partially addressed by config wizard), advanced alert analytics (Q4 candidate), mobile app (not validated by customer demand).

## Exercise 5: Anticipate the pushback

For each theme, write the most likely objection and your response:

**"Why are we spending 40 weeks on three enterprise deals?"** Because those three deals represent 19% of our current ARR. The features — SSO, audit logging — are also prerequisites for every future enterprise deal. This is not a one-time investment; it is unlocking a market segment.

**"Why not just improve documentation instead of building a wizard?"** Documentation helps users who read documentation. Our data shows 70% of trial users never open the docs. A wizard meets them where they are — inside the product.

**"Can we do the monolith split in Q4 instead?"** We could, but the coordination tax compounds. Every week we delay costs 30% velocity across three teams. Starting now means Q4 features ship 30% faster. Delaying means Q4 features ship at the same impaired rate.

**"What if enterprise deals slip?"** We will check milestone progress at Week 4 and Week 8. If the SSO integration is blocked, we will escalate immediately. The SOC 2 evidence collection can run in parallel with sales conversations — we do not need to finish before sales can engage.

## Your turn

Write a quarterly roadmap for Stackline — or for your own product if you have one. Use the structure from this exercise:

1. Define 3-4 themes with business rationale and outcomes
2. Prioritize within at least one theme using RICE
3. Define leading and lagging metrics for each theme
4. Write a one-page executive summary
5. Anticipate and respond to the top three objections

The best way to learn strategy is to practice it. Frameworks are only useful when they are internalized through repetition. Write the roadmap. Read it out loud. Ask yourself: "If I were the CEO, would I fund this?" If the answer is not an immediate yes, revise until it is.

## What to remember

Product strategy is the discipline of allocating scarce engineering resources to maximize business outcomes. The quarterly roadmap is your primary artifact. Themes give it structure. RICE and MoSCoW give it rigor. Metrics give it accountability. Stakeholder communication gives it support. And the willingness to say "here is what we are not doing" gives it credibility. Practice this. Write roadmaps even when nobody asks you to. The CTO who can produce a clear, defensible, well-communicated strategy document is the CTO who earns the trust to execute it.
