## Why this matters

Every product backlog has more ideas than engineering capacity. Without a defensible prioritization system, the loudest voice in the room wins. That produces a product shaped by politics rather than strategy. Frameworks give you a shared language for saying "here is why we are doing A before B" — and, critically, "here is why we are not doing C at all."

## RICE scoring

RICE is one of the most widely used prioritization frameworks. It scores each initiative on four dimensions:

- **Reach** — How many users or accounts will this affect in a given time period?
- **Impact** — How much will it move the metric that matters? (Scored on a scale: 3 = massive, 2 = high, 1 = medium, 0.5 = low, 0.25 = minimal)
- **Confidence** — How sure are you about reach and impact estimates? (100% = high, 80% = medium, 50% = low)
- **Effort** — How many person-months will this take?

The formula:

```
RICE Score = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort
```

Here is a practical example comparing two initiatives:

```
Feature A: Onboarding redesign
  Reach: 5,000 new signups/quarter
  Impact: 2 (high — affects activation rate)
  Confidence: 80%
  Effort: 3 person-months
  RICE = (5000 × 2 × 0.8) / 3 = 2,667

Feature B: Admin dashboard dark mode
  Reach: 200 admin users
  Impact: 0.5 (low — cosmetic preference)
  Confidence: 100%
  Effort: 1 person-month
  RICE = (200 × 0.5 × 1.0) / 1 = 100
```

Feature A scores 26x higher. The math makes the decision obvious in a way that "I think onboarding is more important" never could. RICE is especially powerful because it forces you to estimate **effort**, which prevents the common trap of prioritizing easy wins over high-impact work.

## MoSCoW method

MoSCoW categorizes items into four buckets:

- **Must have** — The product fails without this. Non-negotiable for the release.
- **Should have** — Important but not critical. The product works without it, but users will notice.
- **Could have** — Nice to have. Include if capacity allows.
- **Won't have (this time)** — Explicitly out of scope for this cycle.

The power of MoSCoW is in that last category. Most prioritization systems focus on what you will do. MoSCoW forces you to explicitly state what you will not do — and that is where alignment actually happens.

```
Q3 Release: Self-Serve Onboarding

Must Have:
  - New signup flow with email verification
  - Basic usage dashboard
  - Stripe billing integration

Should Have:
  - In-app product tour
  - Team invite flow

Could Have:
  - Custom onboarding questionnaire
  - Integration marketplace preview

Won't Have (This Time):
  - SSO/SAML support
  - White-label branding
  - Mobile onboarding
```

When a stakeholder pushes for SSO mid-quarter, you can point to the MoSCoW document. SSO was explicitly discussed, evaluated, and deferred. This is not a surprise — it is a decision that was already made.

## Opportunity scoring

Opportunity scoring comes from the Outcome-Driven Innovation framework. It measures two things for each customer need:

1. **Importance** — How important is this outcome to the customer? (1-10)
2. **Satisfaction** — How satisfied are customers with the current solution? (1-10)

The opportunity score formula:

```
Opportunity = Importance + max(Importance - Satisfaction, 0)
```

High importance + low satisfaction = high opportunity. This framework is particularly useful when you have customer research data and want to find underserved needs.

```
Need: "Quickly understand why a deploy failed"
  Importance: 9
  Satisfaction: 3
  Opportunity: 9 + (9 - 3) = 15  ← Strong opportunity

Need: "See a list of all deploys"
  Importance: 7
  Satisfaction: 8
  Opportunity: 7 + max(7 - 8, 0) = 7  ← Already well-served
```

Opportunity scoring prevents you from over-investing in areas where customers are already satisfied. It directs effort toward genuine gaps.

## The art of saying no

Frameworks help, but the hardest part of prioritization is not math — it is the conversation that follows. Saying no to a stakeholder, a customer, or your own CEO requires a specific skill set.

**Never say "no" without saying "because."** Naked rejection breeds resentment. "We are not building SSO this quarter because our churn data shows that reliability, not enterprise features, is the primary driver of lost revenue. SSO is on the Q4 evaluation list."

**Offer the tradeoff, not the refusal.** Instead of "We can't do that," say "We can do that if we drop the product tour from Q3. Here is how that changes the onboarding metrics we committed to." Now the requester owns the tradeoff, not you.

**Distinguish "not now" from "not ever."** Most requests are legitimate — they just do not outrank the current priorities. Acknowledge the validity of the request while being clear about timing. "This is a strong idea. It does not fit in Q3 given our reliability commitment, but I am adding it to the Q4 candidate list with your context attached."

**Document every no.** Keep a running "Not Doing" list with the rationale for each item. This prevents the same request from consuming meeting time every two weeks. It also protects you: when someone claims a need was ignored, you can show exactly when it was discussed and why it was deferred.

## Combining frameworks

No single framework works for every decision. In practice, experienced CTOs layer them:

1. **MoSCoW** at the quarterly level to set the boundaries of the release
2. **RICE** within the "Should Have" and "Could Have" categories to rank individual items
3. **Opportunity scoring** during product discovery to identify where to invest research

The frameworks are not the decision. They are inputs to a conversation. A RICE score of 2,667 does not automatically mean "build it" — it means "this deserves a serious discussion." Your judgment, your understanding of the team, and your knowledge of the competitive landscape are still the final filter.

## Red flags in prioritization

Watch for these patterns — they indicate your prioritization process is broken:

- **Everything is P0.** If everything is the highest priority, nothing is. Force-rank. Accept the discomfort.
- **The HiPPO effect.** The Highest Paid Person's Opinion overrides data. Frameworks exist to counter this. Use them.
- **Recency bias.** The last customer complaint becomes the top priority. One complaint is an anecdote. Ten complaints with the same root cause is a signal.
- **Sunk cost attachment.** "We already spent two months on this, so we have to finish it." No. If the remaining work does not justify its opportunity cost, stop.

## What to remember

Prioritization is resource allocation under uncertainty. RICE gives you quantitative rigor. MoSCoW gives you explicit scope boundaries. Opportunity scoring connects you to customer needs. But the real skill is not running the formula — it is having the conviction to defend the result, especially when the answer is "not now."
