## Why this matters

What you measure shapes what your team optimizes for. Choose the wrong metrics and you get perverse incentives — teams shipping half-finished features to hit a velocity number, or avoiding refactoring because it does not count as "output." Choose the right metrics and you create a feedback loop that naturally drives quality, speed, and user value.

## DORA metrics: the industry standard for engineering performance

The DORA (DevOps Research and Assessment) framework, backed by years of research across thousands of organizations, identifies four key metrics that predict both engineering performance and organizational outcomes.

**Deployment frequency.** How often does your team deploy to production? Elite teams deploy on demand, multiple times per day. Low performers deploy between once per month and once every six months.

**Lead time for changes.** How long from code commit to running in production? Elite teams: less than one hour. Low performers: between one month and six months.

**Change failure rate.** What percentage of deployments cause a failure in production? Elite teams: 0-15%. Low performers: 46-60%.

**Mean time to recover (MTTR).** When a failure occurs, how long until service is restored? Elite teams: less than one hour. Low performers: between one week and one month.

```
DORA Performance Levels:

                Elite      High        Medium      Low
Frequency       On demand  Weekly-     Monthly-    Monthly-
                           monthly     biannually  biannually
Lead Time       < 1 hour   1 day-      1-6 months  1-6 months
                           1 week
Failure Rate    0-15%      16-30%      16-30%      46-60%
MTTR            < 1 hour   < 1 day     1 day-      1 week-
                                       1 week      1 month
```

The power of DORA is that the four metrics are interconnected. You cannot game one without affecting the others. Deploying more frequently with a high failure rate just creates more incidents. Reducing failure rate by deploying less frequently slows your lead time. The only way to improve all four simultaneously is to genuinely improve your engineering practices — better testing, better CI/CD, better architecture, better incident response.

## Product metrics: NPS, DAU/MAU, retention

DORA tells you about engineering health. Product metrics tell you whether what you are building actually matters to users.

**Net Promoter Score (NPS).** Ask users one question: "On a scale of 0-10, how likely are you to recommend this product?" Score calculation:

```
Promoters (9-10):   enthusiastic advocates
Passives (7-8):     satisfied but unenthusiastic
Detractors (0-6):   unhappy, may actively discourage others

NPS = % Promoters - % Detractors
Range: -100 to +100
```

An NPS above 50 is excellent. Above 70 is world-class. Below 0 means you have more detractors than promoters — a serious warning sign. NPS is useful because it captures overall sentiment in a single number that trends over time. Its weakness is that it does not tell you why users feel the way they do — always pair it with a follow-up question.

**DAU/MAU ratio.** Daily Active Users divided by Monthly Active Users measures engagement stickiness. A ratio of 0.5 means that, on average, users engage with your product 15 out of 30 days. For most SaaS products, 0.2 (6 days/month) is healthy. Social products aim for 0.5+.

**Retention curves.** Track what percentage of users are still active after 1 day, 7 days, 30 days, and 90 days. A healthy product has a retention curve that flattens — it reaches a stable percentage of users who keep coming back. A product in trouble has a curve that trends toward zero, meaning eventually everyone leaves.

## Leading vs. lagging indicators

This distinction is one of the most important concepts in measurement.

**Lagging indicators** tell you what already happened. Revenue, churn rate, NPS — these are results. By the time a lagging indicator moves, the cause happened weeks or months ago. They are important for accountability but useless for real-time decision-making.

**Leading indicators** predict what will happen. Code review turnaround time, deployment frequency, feature adoption rate in the first week — these move before the lagging indicators do. They give you time to intervene.

```
Lagging Indicator          Leading Indicator(s)
─────────────────────      ──────────────────────────────
Revenue churn              Support ticket volume
                           NPS trend (last 30 days)
                           Feature adoption rate

Engineering velocity       PR review turnaround time
                           CI pipeline duration
                           Onboarding time for new hires

Product-market fit         Activation rate (first session)
                           DAU/MAU ratio
                           Organic referral rate

System reliability         Deployment frequency
                           Change failure rate
                           Alert-to-incident ratio
```

A CTO who only watches lagging indicators is always reacting. A CTO who tracks leading indicators can course-correct before the lagging indicators go red.

## Building a metrics dashboard

A useful CTO dashboard has three layers:

**Layer 1: Daily glance (5 seconds).** Three to five numbers that tell you whether today is normal or abnormal. Deployment count, open incidents, error rate. Green/yellow/red status. If everything is green, move on with your day.

**Layer 2: Weekly review (15 minutes).** DORA metrics trended over the past 4 weeks. Sprint velocity. PR cycle time. Support ticket volume. Look for trends, not individual data points. A single bad week is noise. Three consecutive bad weeks is a signal.

**Layer 3: Monthly strategy (1 hour).** NPS trend. Retention curves. Revenue per engineer. Tech debt register review. Feature adoption rates. This is where you make investment decisions — shift capacity, reprioritize the roadmap, escalate a risk.

## Metrics pathologies

Every metric can be gamed. Watch for these failure modes:

**Goodhart's Law.** "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." If you reward deployment frequency, teams will deploy empty commits. If you reward velocity points, teams will inflate estimates. Measure multiple things. Let the metrics check each other.

**Vanity metrics.** Total signups, page views, total users — these only go up. They feel good and mean almost nothing. Focus on rates and ratios: activation rate, retention rate, revenue per user. These can go down, which makes them honest.

**Measurement overhead.** If tracking a metric costs more than the insight it provides, stop tracking it. A startup with five engineers does not need a DORA dashboard — the CTO can just look around the room. A team of fifty needs the dashboard because direct observation no longer scales.

**Metric fixation.** Not everything that matters can be measured. Team morale, code elegance, developer experience — these are real and important, but reducing them to a number often destroys the nuance. Use qualitative signals (1:1 conversations, retros, exit interviews) alongside quantitative metrics.

## Choosing your metrics

Start with these questions:

1. What is the most important business outcome for the next quarter? (Pick one.)
2. What lagging indicator measures that outcome?
3. What leading indicators predict that lagging indicator?
4. Can you measure those leading indicators with your current tools?

If the answer to question 4 is no, invest in observability before you invest in optimization. You cannot improve what you cannot see.

## What to remember

DORA metrics measure engineering health. NPS and DAU/MAU measure product health. Leading indicators give you time to act; lagging indicators confirm whether you acted correctly. Build a three-layer dashboard: daily glance, weekly review, monthly strategy. Watch for Goodhart's Law — when a metric becomes a target, pair it with a counter-metric. And remember: the purpose of measurement is insight, not surveillance.
