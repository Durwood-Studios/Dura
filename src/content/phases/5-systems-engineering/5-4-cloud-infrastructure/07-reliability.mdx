# Reliability: SLOs, Error Budgets, and Chaos Engineering

"Our service is reliable" is not an engineering statement — it's a feeling. Reliability without numbers is not something you can improve, budget for, or have an honest conversation with customers about. The Site Reliability Engineering (SRE) discipline, largely pioneered at Google, gave the industry a vocabulary for making reliability concrete and measurable.

The core insight is deceptively simple: 100% reliability is the wrong goal. Pursuing it costs infinite engineering time and prevents you from shipping features. The right question is: how unreliable can we afford to be, and how do we spend that budget intentionally?

## SLIs: What You Measure

An **SLI (Service Level Indicator)** is the actual measurement of a behavior that represents service quality. SLIs are specific, quantitative, and computed from real data. Common SLIs include:

- **Request success rate**: percentage of requests that return a non-5xx response
- **Request latency**: percentage of requests served within a latency threshold (e.g., under 200ms)
- **Availability**: percentage of time the service responds to health checks
- **Error rate**: percentage of requests that result in an error

A good SLI measures something the user actually experiences. Disk I/O throughput is an operational metric; request latency is an SLI.

## SLOs: What You Commit To Internally

An **SLO (Service Level Objective)** is a target value for an SLI — the threshold below which your team considers the service to be failing. SLOs are internal commitments that drive engineering decisions.

Examples of SLOs:

- "99.9% of HTTP requests will succeed" (three nines of success rate)
- "99% of API responses will be delivered in under 500ms"
- "The service will be available 99.95% of the time per rolling 30-day window"

SLOs are not chosen arbitrarily. They should be set based on what users actually need to be happy — not on what sounds impressive. A user of a batch processing job might be perfectly happy with 95% availability. A user of a payment terminal is not.

Three nines (99.9%) sounds impressive but means roughly 8.76 hours of allowable downtime per year. Four nines (99.99%) is about 52 minutes. Five nines (99.999%) is about 5 minutes. Each additional nine is dramatically more expensive to achieve.

## SLAs: What You Promise Externally

An **SLA (Service Level Agreement)** is a contractual commitment made to customers, usually with financial consequences for breach — refunds, service credits, or contract termination. SLAs are always less aggressive than SLOs. If your SLO is 99.9% availability and your SLA promises 99.5%, you have a buffer of 0.4% before you owe customers anything.

SLAs are legal documents. They're written by lawyers and reviewed by legal teams. The engineering team usually sets the SLO, and the business uses the SLO as a basis for what SLAs can be responsibly offered.

## Error Budgets: Reliability as a Resource

An **error budget** is the amount of unreliability you're allowed to consume before breaching your SLO. If your SLO is 99.9% success rate over a 30-day window, and you handled one million requests this month, your error budget is 0.1% × 1,000,000 = 1,000 failed requests.

Error budgets transform reliability into a shared resource that both the development team and the operations team have a stake in. The key insight: if the error budget is mostly unspent, the system has been reliable and the team has "permission" to take risks — deploy more often, experiment with new features, accept occasional instability. If the error budget is nearly exhausted, the team must slow down: freeze deployments, focus on reliability improvements, prioritize stability over features.

This creates a natural feedback loop. Teams that burn error budget too fast from buggy deployments become motivated to improve testing and deployment practices. The error budget is a forcing function for reliability.

## Chaos Engineering

**Chaos engineering** is the practice of deliberately injecting failures into a system in a controlled way to find weaknesses before they cause real outages. The Netflix team pioneered this with Chaos Monkey — a tool that randomly terminated EC2 instances in production to ensure the system could handle instance failures gracefully.

The philosophy: failures in complex distributed systems are inevitable. Rather than hoping your error handling works, you prove it works by testing it under controlled conditions. A system that has survived intentional failures is more reliably resilient than a system that has merely not failed yet.

Modern chaos engineering is disciplined. You form a hypothesis ("the service will continue handling traffic normally if one of three replicas is killed"), define a steady-state baseline (current SLI metrics), run the experiment, and observe whether the hypothesis holds. If it doesn't, you've found a real weakness before a real incident exposed it.

Chaos experiments should start small — a single instance in a staging environment — and expand over time as confidence grows. Running chaos experiments against production without organizational buy-in and rollback procedures is not chaos engineering; it's recklessness.

## Putting It Together

The reliability discipline has a simple structure: SLIs tell you what to measure. SLOs tell you what target to hit. Error budgets tell you how much risk you can take. Chaos engineering lets you verify your resilience before failure finds you.

A team with well-defined SLOs and a functioning error budget system has something most teams lack: a shared, objective, data-driven conversation about when to slow down and invest in stability vs. when to move fast and ship features.
